# 12-Week 10K Training Plan (Beginner Level)

## Plan Overview

**Target Audience:** Runners who have finished a 5K and want to step up to the 10K
**Goal:** Complete and then race a 10K in 12 weeks
**Weekly Mileage:** 14-27 miles (peak 27)
**Running Days:** 4-5 per week (4 on recovery weeks)
**Experience Required:** Finished a 5K, currently running at least 3 times a week, comfortable
running 30-40 minutes continuously

If you have not raced a 5K yet, start with the
[beginner 5K plan](12-week-5k-training-plan-beginner.md). If you are already running 25-30 miles
per week with tempo and interval work in your legs, use the
[intermediate 10K plan](12-week-10k-training-plan.md) instead.

## Before You Start

1. **Determine Your Current Fitness:**
   - Use your recent 5K time. That is enough. You do not need a fresh time trial.
   - Rough conversion: your 10K pace will be about 15-25 seconds per mile slower than your 5K
     pace. Do not overthink this in Week 1.
   - If you have no recent 5K, run a relaxed one in Week 2 or 3 as a benchmark, not as a race.

2. **Know Your Efforts:**
   - **Easy:** Conversational, full sentences. Zone 2, 60-70% max HR. This is most of the plan.
     See [heart-rate zones](../concepts/heart-rate-zones.md).
   - **Steady/Cruise:** Comfortably hard. 3-5 word phrases only. Roughly the pace you could hold
     for an hour. This is the only "hard" effort in the plan until Week 11.
   - **Goal 10K pace:** Introduced late, and only in small doses.
   - Deliberately absent: **VO2 max intervals.** You do not need them yet, and the injury cost of
     adding them at this stage outweighs the benefit. The 10K is primarily a
     [lactate threshold](../concepts/lactate-threshold.md) race. Threshold work is what this plan
     trains.

3. **Schedule Your Race:**
   - Pick your goal 10K for Week 12 (Sunday).
   - Optional: a 5K tune-up race in Week 8.

## Training Philosophy

The 10K is roughly double the 5K in distance and roughly triple it in respect. The distance is
not the hard part; the temptation to train it like a 5K is. Beginners stepping up to the 10K get
hurt for one reason above all others: they add both mileage and intensity at the same time.

This plan does not.

### The Non-Negotiables

1. **Consistency beats intensity.** Missing a full week costs more than any single easy run being
   too slow. Ever. Protect the habit before you protect the workout.
2. **Easy means EASY.** The single most common error at this stage is running easy days too hard.
   You are stepping up your volume; if your easy days stay at 5K-training effort, the volume will
   break you. Slow the easy days down. See [the easy/hard rule](../concepts/easy-hard.md) and
   [the aerobic system](../fundamentals/aerobic-system.md).
3. **Build volume BEFORE intensity.** Volume rises for four weeks before the first cruise
   interval. Never raise both in the same week.
4. **Every 4th week is a down week.** Weeks 4 and 8 cut volume roughly 25-27%. Adaptation happens
   in the easy weeks. See [recovery](../fundamentals/recovery.md).
5. **Rest days are training.** Two per week, all plan long. They are not padding, they are not a
   sign of weakness, and they do not need to be earned.
6. **Gentle tempo only.** Cruise intervals and steady running, not track intervals. You will do
   one small dose of goal-10K-pace running before the race and that is all.

### Phase Map

- **Weeks 1-4:** Base. All easy, plus strides. Get used to 5 running days.
- **Weeks 5-8:** Build. Cruise intervals introduced, volume climbing.
- **Weeks 9-11:** Sharpen. Sustained tempo, then a goal-pace rehearsal in the long run.
- **Week 12:** Taper and race.

## The Weekly Template

- **Monday:** Rest
- **Tuesday:** Quality day (easy in Phase 1, cruise/tempo from Week 5)
- **Wednesday:** Easy run (short)
- **Thursday:** Easy run
- **Friday:** Rest or cross-train
- **Saturday:** Long run
- **Sunday:** Recovery run (very short and very easy) or rest

Note the two hard-ish anchors of the week are Tuesday and Saturday, separated by three days in
each direction. That spacing is deliberate. Do not compress it.

## The 12-Week Plan

### Phase 1: Base Building (Weeks 1-4)

No quality work at all. If you finish a run in this phase feeling like you worked hard, you ran
it wrong.

#### Week 1: Establish Base (16 miles)
Four running days this week. The fifth day arrives in Week 2.
- **Monday:** Rest
- **Tuesday:** Easy 4 miles
- **Wednesday:** Easy 3 miles
- **Thursday:** Easy 4 miles + 4x100m strides
- **Friday:** Rest or 30 min cross-train
- **Saturday:** Long run 5 miles easy
- **Sunday:** Rest

#### Week 2: Add the Fifth Day (17 miles)
- **Monday:** Rest
- **Tuesday:** Easy 4 miles
- **Wednesday:** Easy 3 miles
- **Thursday:** Easy 3 miles + 6x100m strides
- **Friday:** Rest or 30 min cross-train
- **Saturday:** Long run 5 miles easy
- **Sunday:** Recovery 2 miles (very easy, shorter and slower than you think)

#### Week 3: Extend the Long Run (18 miles)
- **Monday:** Rest
- **Tuesday:** Easy 4 miles + 6x100m strides
- **Wednesday:** Easy 3 miles
- **Thursday:** Easy 3 miles
- **Friday:** Rest or 30-40 min cross-train
- **Saturday:** Long run 6 miles easy
- **Sunday:** Recovery 2 miles

#### Week 4: Recovery Week (14 miles)
Back to four running days. This is not a lost week; it is the week the previous three start
paying out.
- **Monday:** Rest
- **Tuesday:** Easy 4 miles
- **Wednesday:** Easy 3 miles
- **Thursday:** Easy 3 miles + 6x100m strides
- **Friday:** Rest
- **Saturday:** Long run 4 miles easy
- **Sunday:** Rest

---

### Phase 2: Build - Introduce Cruise Work (Weeks 5-8)

Now, and only now, does anything get faster than easy. One quality session a week, on Tuesday.
Everything else stays conversational.

#### Week 5: First Cruise Intervals (19 miles)
"Comfortably hard" means 3-5 word phrases. If you can only manage single words, slow down.
- **Monday:** Rest
- **Tuesday:** **Cruise Intervals** - 1.5mi warmup + 2x6 min comfortably hard w/2 min easy jog + 1.5mi cooldown = 5 miles
- **Wednesday:** Easy 3 miles
- **Thursday:** Easy 3 miles + 6x100m strides
- **Friday:** Rest or 30-40 min cross-train
- **Saturday:** Long run 6 miles easy
- **Sunday:** Recovery 2 miles

#### Week 6: Build the Cruise (20 miles)
- **Monday:** Rest
- **Tuesday:** **Cruise Intervals** - 2mi warmup + 3x6 min comfortably hard w/2 min easy jog + 2mi cooldown = 6 miles
- **Wednesday:** Easy 3 miles
- **Thursday:** Easy 3 miles + 6x100m strides
- **Friday:** Rest or 30-40 min cross-train
- **Saturday:** Long run 6 miles easy
- **Sunday:** Recovery 2 miles

#### Week 7: Longer Cruise Reps (22 miles)
- **Monday:** Rest
- **Tuesday:** **Cruise Intervals** - 2mi warmup + 2x10 min comfortably hard w/3 min easy jog + 2mi cooldown = 6 miles
- **Wednesday:** Easy 3 miles
- **Thursday:** Easy 4 miles + 6x100m strides
- **Friday:** Rest
- **Saturday:** Long run 7 miles easy
- **Sunday:** Recovery 2 miles

#### Week 8: Recovery + Optional Tune-Up (16 miles)
- **Monday:** Rest
- **Tuesday:** Easy 4 miles
- **Wednesday:** Easy 3 miles
- **Thursday:** Easy 3 miles + 6x100m strides
- **Friday:** Rest
- **Saturday:** **OPTIONAL: 5K tune-up race** OR long run 4 miles easy
- **Sunday:** Recovery 2 miles

If you race the 5K, warm up 1 mile and cool down 1 mile around it, and drop Sunday's recovery run
if your legs are complaining. The result gives you a realistic goal 10K pace: add roughly 15-25
seconds per mile to your 5K pace.

---

### Phase 3: Sharpen - Sustained Threshold (Weeks 9-11)

Volume peaks and the tempo work becomes continuous. Still only one quality session per week.

#### Week 9: Continuous Tempo (24 miles)
- **Monday:** Rest
- **Tuesday:** **Tempo** - 2mi warmup + 20 min continuous at comfortably hard + 2mi cooldown = 7 miles
- **Wednesday:** Easy 4 miles
- **Thursday:** Easy 4 miles + 6x100m strides
- **Friday:** Rest or 30-40 min cross-train
- **Saturday:** Long run 7 miles easy (last mile steady, not hard)
- **Sunday:** Recovery 2 miles

#### Week 10: Peak Cruise Volume (26 miles)
- **Monday:** Rest
- **Tuesday:** **Cruise Intervals** - 2mi warmup + 3x8 min comfortably hard w/2 min easy jog + 2mi cooldown = 7 miles
- **Wednesday:** Easy 4 miles
- **Thursday:** Easy 5 miles + 6x100m strides
- **Friday:** Rest
- **Saturday:** Long run 8 miles easy
- **Sunday:** Recovery 2 miles

#### Week 11: Peak Week + Race-Pace Rehearsal (27 miles)
The long run is where you find out whether your goal pace is real. Run the first 7 miles easy,
then hold goal 10K pace for the last 2. If you cannot, your goal is too fast. Change the goal,
not the plan.
- **Monday:** Rest
- **Tuesday:** **Tempo** - 2mi warmup + 15 min continuous at comfortably hard + 2mi cooldown = 6 miles
- **Wednesday:** Easy 4 miles
- **Thursday:** Easy 5 miles + 6x100m strides
- **Friday:** Rest
- **Saturday:** **Long run 9 miles** - 7 miles easy + last 2 miles at goal 10K pace
- **Sunday:** Recovery 3 miles

---

### Phase 4: Taper + Race (Week 12)

#### Week 12: Taper Week (12 miles + race)
- **Monday:** Rest
- **Tuesday:** Easy 4 miles + 6x100m strides
- **Wednesday:** **Sharpener** - 1mi easy + 10 min at goal 10K pace + 1mi easy = 3 miles
- **Thursday:** Easy 3 miles
- **Friday:** Rest
- **Saturday:** Easy 2 miles shakeout + 4x100m strides
- **Sunday:** **RACE DAY! 10K Goal Race** 🏁

Volume drops by more than half. You will feel heavy-legged and twitchy mid-week. That is the
taper working, not fitness disappearing. Nothing you run this week can make you fitter; several
things can make you slower.

## Workout Descriptions

### Easy Runs
- **Pace:** Conversational, full sentences
- **HR:** Zone 2 (60-70% max)
- **Purpose:** Build the aerobic base that a 10K is actually made of
- **Note:** Should feel genuinely easy, even "too easy." When you step up in mileage, this stops
  being optional.

### Recovery Runs
- **Pace:** Slower than your easy pace. Deliberately, almost embarrassingly slow.
- **Purpose:** Blood flow and gentle volume the day after the long run
- **Note:** If a recovery run leaves you tired, skip it and take the rest day instead. It is the
  first run to drop in any week, always.

### Strides
- **Distance:** 100 meters (about 20 seconds)
- **Pace:** Relaxed acceleration to roughly mile race pace, never all-out
- **Recovery:** Walk back to the start
- **Purpose:** Form, leg speed, and neuromuscular sharpness without the cost of hard intervals
- **Note:** You should finish a set of strides feeling looser, not tired.

### Cruise Intervals
- **Pace:** Comfortably hard, broken into repeats with short jog recoveries
- **HR:** Zone 4 (roughly 80-88% max)
- **Feel:** 3-5 word phrases
- **Purpose:** Raise your [lactate threshold](../concepts/lactate-threshold.md), which is the
  single biggest lever on a 10K time
- **Note:** Broken into reps precisely so a beginner can hold the right effort. The short
  recoveries are not a rest, they are a reset.

### Tempo Runs
- **Pace:** Same effort as cruise intervals, but continuous
- **Purpose:** Teach the body and the head to sustain threshold effort
- **Note:** Start conservatively. A tempo that finishes strong is a success; one that falls apart
  in the last 5 minutes was run too fast.

### Goal 10K Pace
- **Pace:** Your target race pace, and no faster
- **Purpose:** Rehearsal. Learn what the pace feels like on tired legs so it is familiar on race
  day.
- **Note:** Small doses only. Racing your race in training wins nothing.

### Long Runs
- **Pace:** Easy, except where the plan explicitly says otherwise (Weeks 9 and 11)
- **Purpose:** Endurance, and the confidence that 6.2 miles is comfortably inside your range
- **Note:** Time on feet matters more than pace. Your longest run reaches 9 miles, comfortably
  more than race distance. That is on purpose.

## If You Miss a Week

**Missed 1-3 days:** Nothing. Resume at today's date on the calendar. Do not compress the missed
runs into the days you have left.

**Missed 1 week:** Repeat the week you missed, then continue. Your plan is now 13 weeks. If the
race date is fixed, skip forward instead but cut the next week's volume by about 20% and convert
that week's quality session to an easy run.

**Missed 2 weeks:** Drop back 2 weeks in the plan and resume there. Spend 2 weeks rebuilding
easy volume before you touch quality again. Coming back to cruise intervals at your pre-break
volume is the classic way to turn a two-week break into a two-month one.

**Missed 3+ weeks:** Restart from Week 1 if it was injury or illness, or from Week 5 if life
simply got in the way and you feel fine. Re-set the race goal to "finish strong" rather than a
time. That is a real goal.

**Missed a single session, not a week:**
1. **Missed easy or recovery run:** Skip it. It is gone.
2. **Missed long run:** Move it within the week if you can, keeping at least one easy day
   between it and Tuesday's quality session.
3. **Missed quality session:** Do it 1-2 days later, or skip it entirely. Never move it next to
   the long run.
4. **Never:** Stack two hard days back to back to catch up.

## Signs You Should Back Off

You are stepping up in volume, which is exactly when these signals matter most. Back off if you
see any of them:

- **Pain that changes your gait.** Soreness is normal. Limping is information.
- **Pain that worsens as the run goes on** instead of easing off after 10 minutes.
- **Sharp, localized, or one-sided pain** in the shin, knee, hip, or top of the foot. Stop
  running now, not next week. See [injury prevention](../fundamentals/injury-prevention.md).
- **Resting heart rate up 5+ bpm** for several consecutive mornings.
- **Easy runs feeling hard** at your normal easy pace. This is fatigue, not lost fitness.
- **Sleep degrading** as mileage climbs.
- **Cruise intervals you cannot finish** at the pace you started them.
- **Loss of motivation.** One of the earliest and most reliable overreaching signals there is.
- **Repeated minor illness.**

**What "backing off" means:** take 2-3 extra rest days, or cut the week's volume in half and drop
all quality work. Then resume at the *previous* week's volume, not the one you missed. The cost of
backing off early is a few days. The cost of pushing through is measured in months.

## Race Week Strategy

### Days Before Race:
- **5 days out:** Last touch of goal pace (Wednesday sharpener). Nothing hard after this.
- **3-4 days out:** Short easy running only.
- **2 days out:** Rest.
- **1 day out:** Short shakeout with a few strides, or rest. Both are fine.

### Race Day Warm-Up:
- 10-15 min easy jog
- 4-6x100m strides, building to race pace
- Finish the warm-up 10 minutes before the start

### Race Pacing Strategy:
The 10K punishes an over-fast start more than any other common race distance. You have roughly
one mile of margin for error and you will spend it in the first 800 meters if you are not careful.

- **Miles 1-2:** Deliberately controlled. It should feel too easy. People will stream past you.
- **Miles 3-4:** Settle into goal pace. This is where the race is decided and where it starts to
  feel like work.
- **Miles 5-6:** Hold on, then lift if you can. If you paced the first half properly, you will be
  passing people here.
- **Last 0.2:** Empty the tank.

### After Race:
- Cool down with 10 min easy jogging or a walk.
- Take 5-7 days easy or off. You have earned it.
- No hard workouts for at least a week.

## Weekly Mileage Progression

```
Week 1:  16 miles (base, 4 days)
Week 2:  17 miles (base, 5 days)
Week 3:  18 miles (base)
Week 4:  14 miles (RECOVERY)
Week 5:  19 miles (first cruise intervals)
Week 6:  20 miles (build cruise)
Week 7:  22 miles (longer cruise reps)
Week 8:  16 miles (RECOVERY / optional 5K tune-up)
Week 9:  24 miles (continuous tempo)
Week 10: 26 miles (peak cruise volume)
Week 11: 27 miles (PEAK, goal-pace rehearsal)
Week 12: 12 miles + RACE (taper)
```

No week rises more than about 10% over the previous build week, and the long run never exceeds
about a third of weekly volume. Both of those constraints are load-bearing. If you "feel great"
and want to break them, that feeling is exactly what they exist to protect you from.

## Training Intensity Distribution

- **~85-90% easy running** (Zones 1-2)
- **~10-15% threshold/cruise** (Zone 4)
- **0% VO2 max intervals** - deliberately

That is a heavier easy share than the intermediate plan, and it is correct for this stage. Your
aerobic base is the limiter, not your top-end speed. Fix the limiter.

## Key Differences from the Beginner 5K Plan

- **More running days:** 5 instead of 4
- **More weekly mileage:** peak 27 vs peak 19
- **Longer long runs:** up to 9 miles vs 6
- **Tempo/threshold, not intervals:** the 10K is a threshold race
- **No run/walk assumption:** you should be running these continuously by now, though walk breaks
  on the long run remain entirely legitimate

## Key Takeaways

1. **Consistency beats intensity.** A missed week costs more than a hundred too-slow easy runs.
2. **Easy means easy.** Stepping up mileage only works if the easy days get slower, not faster.
3. **Volume first, intensity second, never both at once.**
4. **The 10K is a threshold race.** Cruise intervals and tempo runs are the workouts that matter.
   Track intervals can wait until you have another training cycle behind you.
5. **Down weeks and rest days are when you actually adapt.**
6. **Back off early and cheaply**, rather than late and expensively.
