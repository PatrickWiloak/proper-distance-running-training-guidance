# 20-Week Marathon Training Plan (Beginner Level)

## Plan Overview

**Target Audience:** First-time marathoners. You have been running consistently for 6+ months, you are comfortable running 5-6 miles, and ideally you have finished a half marathon or a long training run of 10+ miles.
**Goal:** Finish your first marathon (26.2 miles) healthy, and still be *running* at mile 22
**Weekly Mileage:** 18-40 miles (peak 40)
**Running Days:** 4-5 per week
**Peak Long Run:** 20 miles (three long runs of 18-20)
**Taper:** 3 full weeks
**Why 20 weeks and not 18:** A first marathon needs a longer runway. The [intermediate 18-week plan](18-week-marathon-training-plan.md) assumes you arrive with a 35-40 mile base. This one assumes you do not, and spends the extra weeks building it safely.

## The Only Goal That Matters

**The goal of a first marathon is to FINISH HEALTHY. Time goals are for the second one.**

Say it out loud. Write it on your hand on race morning if you have to.

Every bad first-marathon story starts the same way: a beginner picks a time goal from a pace calculator, trains slightly too hard for it, arrives at the start line tired, goes out at that pace anyway, and detonates at mile 19. They walk-shuffle the last 7 miles, hate every minute of it, and finish 40 minutes slower than they would have if they had simply started 30 seconds per mile slower and run their own race.

You do not have a marathon time. You have never run one. You cannot know what your marathon pace is until you have run a marathon. So the first one is the measurement, not the exam.

Finish healthy, learn what 26.2 miles actually feels like, and then - if you want - go chase a time on your second one with real data and a real base.

## Before You Start

1. **Establish Your Baseline:**
   - Running consistently 4 days a week for at least 2-3 months.
   - Currently running 15-20 miles per week.
   - Have completed a run of at least 8-10 miles in the last 6 weeks.
   - If you are not here yet, spend 8-12 weeks getting here first. Do not start this plan on a hope. There is no version of marathon training that survives a shaky base.

2. **Medical Clearance:**
   - Marathon training is a genuine physiological load. If you are over 40, sedentary until recently, carrying extra weight, or have any cardiac/metabolic history, get cleared by a physician first.
   - This is not boilerplate. Do it.

3. **Know Your Paces (you need very few):**
   - **Easy:** Conversational, full sentences. Zone 2, 60-70% max HR. This is 80%+ of your running.
   - **Long-run pace:** SLOWER than easy feels necessary. See below.
   - **Steady:** Controlled and firm, sustainable for about an hour. Zone 3-low Zone 4. A small dose, late in the plan.
   - You do not need VO2max intervals, track sessions, or a VDOT score to finish a marathon. You need an aerobic engine and 20 weeks of patience. See [heart-rate zones](../concepts/heart-rate-zones.md).

4. **Decide Now: Continuous or Run/Walk?**
   - Read the run/walk section below **before** Week 1, not in Week 18. It changes how you execute the plan and it is a completely legitimate way to run a first marathon.

5. **Schedule Your Race:**
   - Pick a marathon 20 weeks out. Register.
   - Optional tune-up half marathon around Week 12-13 - run it as a hard training run, not a race.

## Training Philosophy

The marathon is an aerobic event with a fuel-management problem attached. For a first-timer, nearly all of the required adaptation comes from consistent easy aerobic running and a progressively longer, *slow* long run. Speedwork contributes almost nothing to your ability to finish 26.2 miles, and it contributes a great deal to your ability to get injured before you get there.

### The Five Non-Negotiables

**1. Finish healthy. Time goals are for the second one.**
Covered above. It governs every other decision in this plan.

**2. Easy means EASY.**
This is the principle beginners break most often, and in a marathon build it is fatal. Twenty weeks of easy runs at "moderate" pace produces a runner who is chronically fatigued, whose long runs get progressively worse instead of better, and who arrives at the start line already worn down. If you cannot speak a full sentence, you are running too fast. Slow down. Slow down again. See [the easy/hard rule](../concepts/easy-hard.md).

**3. Long runs are run SLOW - slower than goal pace, not at it.**
This is the most counterintuitive instruction in the plan, so here is the reasoning.

Your long run's job is to spend time on your feet. The adaptations you need - more mitochondria, denser capillary beds, better fat oxidation, tougher connective tissue, a gut that tolerates fuel while running - come from **duration under aerobic load**, not from speed. They come from *how long you run*, not *how fast*.

Running your long runs at goal marathon pace turns each of them into a race. It converts a run you should recover from in 2 days into a run you need 5 days to recover from, and it wrecks the following week. Repeat that for 20 weeks and you arrive at the start line broken.

**Run your long runs 60-90 seconds per mile slower than your goal marathon pace.** Yes, really. Yes, even the 20-milers. Especially the 20-milers. See [the aerobic system](../fundamentals/aerobic-system.md).

**4. Build volume before intensity. Never raise both in the same week.**
This plan builds mileage for 12 weeks with essentially zero hard running. Modest steady effort appears only in Weeks 13-17, after the volume is already in place. In any given week, either the miles go up or the effort goes up. Never both.

**5. Every 4th week is a down week.**
Weeks 4, 8, 12, and 16 cut volume by 20-30%. Fitness is built when you recover from training, not while you do it. The down week is not a reward for good behaviour - it is the mechanism by which the previous three weeks turn into fitness. Skipping it does not make you fitter, it makes you injured. See [recovery](../fundamentals/recovery.md).

**Training Phases:**
- **Weeks 1-4:** Foundation (consistency, get the body used to 4-5 runs a week)
- **Weeks 5-12:** Base building (grow the long run to 16 miles, all easy)
- **Weeks 13-17:** Peak (the three big long runs, small dose of steady work, race-specific practice)
- **Weeks 18-20:** Three-week taper and race

## Run/Walk Is a Legitimate Way to Run Your First Marathon

Let us be direct about this, because a lot of plans will not be.

**Running your first marathon with planned walk breaks (the Galloway method) is a completely legitimate, evidence-supported finishing strategy, and for many first-timers it is the *better* one.** Not a lesser one. Not a fallback. A strategy.

**How it works:** you run for a set interval and walk for a set interval, from mile 1, *before* you are tired. Common ratios for a first marathon:
- Run 4 min / walk 1 min
- Run 8 min / walk 1 min
- Run 1 mile / walk 1 minute

**Why it works:**
- The walk breaks come *before* fatigue accumulates, not after it forces them on you. This is the entire point and it is why "I will just walk if I need to" is not the same thing.
- Cycling the muscles between running and walking distributes load, delays the deep muscle damage that causes the late-race shuffle, and keeps your heart rate lower for the same distance.
- Many run/walk marathoners finish *faster* than they would running continuously, because they do not blow up. The 60 seconds you spend walking at mile 8 is repaid many times over by not walking involuntarily for the last 6 miles.
- It dramatically reduces the risk that mile 20 becomes a death march.

**The rules if you choose it:**
1. **Decide before the race, not during it.** Walking because you planned to is a strategy. Walking because you have to is a collapse.
2. **Practice it on every single long run in this plan.** Run/walk is a skill and your legs need to rehearse the transitions. Do not improvise it on race day.
3. **Start the walk breaks from mile 1**, when you feel fresh and it feels ridiculous. That is exactly when they do the most good.
4. **Walk with purpose.** A brisk walk, not a stroll. Then start running again on schedule, even when you do not feel like it.
5. **Use a timer** (watch interval alerts) so you do not have to think about it.

There is no medal category for "ran the whole way". Nobody at the finish line asks. **Finishing strong at mile 22 with planned walk breaks beats crawling at mile 22 having run every step until you could not.**

Everything in the schedule below works identically whether you run continuously or run/walk. If you run/walk, the mileage numbers are still your mileage numbers - the time on feet just gets distributed differently.

## The 20-Week Plan

**Weekly template:**
- **Monday:** Rest or cross-train (30-45 min easy bike, swim, or walk)
- **Tuesday:** Easy run
- **Wednesday:** Easy run (becomes a mid-long run in later weeks)
- **Thursday:** Easy run
- **Friday:** Rest
- **Saturday:** Long run (SLOW)
- **Sunday:** Recovery run (very easy) or rest

Move days to fit your life. Two constraints: keep a rest day immediately before the long run, and never put two demanding days back to back.

### Phase 3: Peak (Weeks 13-17)

The three big long runs live here: 18, 20, and 20. This is where you become a marathoner. It is also the phase where the injury risk is highest, so the surrounding days stay short and easy on purpose.

#### Week 13: Rebuild and Add Steady (35 miles)
- **Monday:** Rest or cross-train
- **Tuesday:** Easy 6 miles
- **Wednesday:** **Steady** - 1mi easy warmup + 4mi at steady effort + 1mi easy cooldown = 6 miles
- **Thursday:** Easy 4 miles
- **Friday:** Rest
- **Saturday:** Long run 16 miles easy
- **Sunday:** Recovery 3 miles very easy

#### Week 14: First Big One (37 miles)
- **Monday:** Rest or cross-train
- **Tuesday:** Easy 6 miles
- **Wednesday:** **Mid-long** - Easy 6 miles
- **Thursday:** Easy 4 miles
- **Friday:** Rest
- **Saturday:** **LONG RUN 18 MILES** - slow, fully fuelled, race kit, race shoes, race breakfast. This is a dress rehearsal.
- **Sunday:** Recovery 3 miles very easy

#### Week 15: The Twenty (39 miles)
- **Monday:** Rest or cross-train
- **Tuesday:** Easy 6 miles
- **Wednesday:** **Steady** - 1mi easy warmup + 4mi at steady effort + 1mi easy cooldown = 6 miles
- **Thursday:** Easy 4 miles
- **Friday:** Rest
- **Saturday:** **LONG RUN 20 MILES** - the big one. Run it SLOW. 60-90 sec/mile slower than goal pace. If you are checking your watch and worrying about pace, you have misunderstood the run.
- **Sunday:** Recovery 3 miles very easy

#### Week 16: Down Week (28 miles)
- **Monday:** Rest
- **Tuesday:** Easy 5 miles
- **Wednesday:** Easy 5 miles + 6x100m strides
- **Thursday:** Easy 4 miles
- **Friday:** Rest
- **Saturday:** Long run 12 miles easy
- **Sunday:** Recovery 2 miles very easy

#### Week 17: Peak Week (40 miles - PEAK MILEAGE)
- **Monday:** Rest or cross-train
- **Tuesday:** Easy 6 miles
- **Wednesday:** **Mid-long** - Easy 6 miles
- **Thursday:** Easy 5 miles
- **Friday:** Rest
- **Saturday:** **FINAL LONG RUN - 20 MILES** (first 17 easy, last 3 at goal marathon pace, purely to feel the contrast). Complete dress rehearsal: same shoes, same socks, same kit, same breakfast, same gels, same timing.
- **Sunday:** Recovery 3 miles very easy

**This is the last hard day of the plan. From here, everything gets easier and your only job is to arrive at the start line fresh.**

---

### Phase 4: Three-Week Taper and Race (Weeks 18-20)

The taper is where beginners talk themselves into disaster. You will feel restless, twitchy, mildly unfit, and convinced you should squeeze in "one more twenty". **You should not.** The training is done. The hay is in the barn.

Fitness takes 3-4 weeks to decay. Fatigue takes 2-3 weeks to clear. The entire purpose of the taper is to let the second thing happen while the first thing does not.

#### Week 18: Taper Begins (31 miles - down 22%)
- **Monday:** Rest or cross-train
- **Tuesday:** Easy 6 miles
- **Wednesday:** **Race-pace practice** - 1mi easy + 3mi at goal marathon pace + 1mi easy = 5 miles
- **Thursday:** Easy 4 miles
- **Friday:** Rest
- **Saturday:** Long run 14 miles easy
- **Sunday:** Recovery 2 miles very easy

#### Week 19: Taper Continues (24 miles - down 23%)
- **Monday:** Rest
- **Tuesday:** Easy 5 miles + 6x100m strides
- **Wednesday:** Easy 4 miles
- **Thursday:** Easy 3 miles
- **Friday:** Rest
- **Saturday:** Long run 10 miles easy (last long run - do not add miles, do not add pace)
- **Sunday:** Recovery 2 miles very easy

#### Week 20: Race Week (14 miles + race)
- **Monday:** Easy 4 miles
- **Tuesday:** Easy 4 miles + 6x100m strides
- **Wednesday:** Easy 3 miles
- **Thursday:** Rest
- **Friday:** Rest
- **Saturday:** Easy 3 miles with 4x100m strides (shakeout), OR rest if you travelled
- **Sunday:** **RACE DAY - MARATHON (26.2 miles)**

### "Why is my long run half my weekly mileage?"

Because it has to be, and that is fine for a first marathon.

Your 20-mile long run sits inside a 39-40 mile week, so it is roughly half your volume. Textbook guidance says the long run should be 30-40% of weekly mileage - to hit that with a 20-miler, you would need to run 50-60 miles a week. **For a first-time marathoner, running 55 miles a week is a far bigger injury risk than running one long slow 20-miler inside a 40-mile week.**

So this plan makes the trade deliberately, and pays for it in three ways:
1. **The long runs are run genuinely slow.** A 20-miler at true easy pace is a very different stress from a 20-miler at goal pace. This is why the "run it slow" rule is not negotiable here.
2. **The days around the long run are short.** Friday is rest, Sunday is a 2-3 mile shuffle.
3. **You get a down week every 4th week** and only three long runs of 18+, not six.

If you come back for a second marathon with a season of running behind you, raise the weekly volume and the ratio fixes itself. For now, this is the defensible version.

### "Why don't I ever run 26.2 in training?"

Because nobody does, including elites. A 26-mile training run would cost you 2-3 weeks of recovery and teach you nothing that 20 miles does not. The last 6.2 miles are covered by the taper (you arrive rested, not tired), the carb-load, the crowd, and race-day adrenaline. Trust it - it genuinely works.

## Workout Descriptions

### Easy Runs (about 85% of this plan)
- **Pace:** Conversational, full sentences. If you are breathing hard, slow down.
- **HR:** Zone 2 (60-70% max)
- **Purpose:** Build the aerobic engine. This is where a marathon comes from.
- **It will feel embarrassingly slow.** Good. That means you are doing it right.

### Recovery Runs
- **Pace:** Slower than easy. A shuffle is fine and often correct.
- **Purpose:** Blood flow and habit, not fitness.
- **The day after a long run.** If your legs are wrecked, walk instead or take the day fully off. Nothing is lost.

### Strides
- **Distance:** 100m, smooth acceleration to fast-but-relaxed, then walk back and fully recover.
- **Purpose:** Form and leg turnover. They are not sprints and should never leave you breathless.

### Steady Runs (Weeks 13, 15 only)
- **Pace:** Controlled and firm - an effort you could hold for roughly an hour. About half-marathon effort.
- **HR:** Zone 3 to low Zone 4 (75-85% max)
- **Purpose:** A modest lift to your [lactate threshold](../concepts/lactate-threshold.md), and a lesson in what "sustainably firm" feels like.
- **If in doubt, run it easier.** A steady run done too easy costs a beginner nothing. Done too hard, it costs the next long run.

### Long Runs (the whole plan, really)
- **Pace:** SLOW. 60-90 seconds per mile slower than goal marathon pace.
- **Purpose:** Time on feet, aerobic adaptation, fueling practice, gut training, mental rehearsal.
- **Walking breaks are entirely acceptable** - and mandatory practice if you are using run/walk.
- **Never race your long run.** If you finish a long run feeling like you raced it, you did, and the next week will show it.

### Marathon Pace (MP) Segments
- Only appear twice: 3 miles at the end of the Week 17 long run, and a 3-mile block in Week 18.
- **Purpose:** to let you *feel* the pace, not to train it.
- A first-timer does not need extensive MP work. What you need is the aerobic capacity to still be running at mile 22.

## Fueling and Hydration

**Practice race-day fueling on EVERY long run over about 90 minutes. Nothing new on race day. Not one thing.**

For most beginners, 90 minutes lands around the 10-mile long run (Week 6). From there, every single long run is a fueling rehearsal. There are no exceptions to this and the marathoners who ignore it are the ones vomiting at mile 18.

**The marathon is the only common race distance where you *cannot* finish well on your stored fuel alone.** You carry roughly 1,500-2,000 calories of muscle and liver glycogen. A marathon costs 2,500-3,000. That gap - the arithmetic of "the wall" - is why fueling is a training subject and not an afterthought.

**Practice this, from Week 6 onward:**
- **Before:** Your exact race-morning breakfast, 2-3 hours before the run. Same food, same amount, same timing. Test it until it is boring.
- **During:** 30-60g of carbohydrate per hour, starting at roughly 45 minutes in - *before* you feel you need it. Typically one gel every 30-45 minutes. Take it with water, not with sports drink.
- **Hydration:** Drink to thirst. Overdrinking is a real and dangerous mistake in marathons (hyponatremia), not a safe default.
- **Your gut is trainable.** The reason you take gels on every long run is partly to practice, and partly to physically adapt the gut to absorbing carbohydrate while running. That adaptation takes months, which is why it starts in Week 6 and not Week 18.
- **After:** Carbohydrate plus protein within 60 minutes.

**Race day:** the exact products, the exact schedule, the exact breakfast you rehearsed. If the race hands out a brand you never trained with, carry your own.

Full detail: [Fueling and Nutrition](../fundamentals/fueling-and-nutrition.md).

## Signs You Should Back Off

Twenty weeks is a long time and the load is real. Learn to read these.

**Stop and back off immediately (2-3 days easy or fully off):**
- **Pain that changes how you run.** Any limp, any altered gait, any compensation. Non-negotiable. Stop.
- **Sharp or point-specific pain**, especially bone pain in the shin, foot, or hip. General muscle soreness is normal. A spot that hurts when you press it is not.
- **Pain that worsens as the run continues** instead of warming up and easing.
- **Any pain that persists more than 48 hours after a run.**
- **Resting heart rate elevated 7+ beats for several consecutive mornings.**

**Cut the week's volume 25-30% and reassess:**
- Easy runs feel hard at paces that were comfortable two weeks ago.
- Your long runs are getting *worse*, not better, as the plan progresses. This is the classic signal that your easy days are too fast.
- Disrupted sleep, appetite loss, low mood, unusual irritability.
- Repeated minor illness (colds that keep coming back).
- Persistent heavy legs every day, not just after long runs.
- You are dreading runs you used to enjoy.

**The rule:** three days off costs you nothing across a 20-week plan. Running through a stress reaction turns a 3-day problem into a 3-month one and ends your race. **Consistency over 20 weeks beats heroism in any single week.** See [injury prevention](../fundamentals/injury-prevention.md).

## If You Miss Runs, a Week, or Get Injured

**If you miss a single easy run:** Skip it. Do not make it up, do not move the miles elsewhere. It genuinely does not matter.

**If you miss a long run:** Move it to Sunday if you can. If the week is lost, do not run the missed long run and the next (longer) one on consecutive weekends - repeat the previous long-run distance instead of jumping ahead.

**If you miss one week:**
- Return at roughly 70-80% of the volume you were running before the break.
- Repeat the week you missed rather than jumping to where the calendar says you are.
- You lost almost no fitness in seven days. You lost seven days of *schedule*. Fix the schedule, not by cramming, but by accepting a slightly smaller peak.

**If you miss two to three weeks:**
- Rebuild for 2 weeks at 60-70% of your previous volume, with no steady work at all.
- Then rejoin the plan about 3-4 weeks earlier than the calendar says.
- If that means you can only fit two long runs of 16-18 miles instead of three of 18-20, **take that trade and keep the full 3-week taper.** An under-trained runner with fresh legs finishes a marathon. An over-crammed one does not start it.

**If you miss four or more weeks:**
- Do not compress the plan. This is the single most reliable way for a beginner to get badly injured.
- Seriously consider deferring to a later marathon. Most races allow it, and there is always another marathon. There is only one of your left femur.
- If you must race, drop to a run/walk strategy, drop any time expectation entirely, and accept that the day is about finishing.

**If you get injured:**
- The plan is now irrelevant. Rehab is the plan.
- See a physiotherapist or sports doctor for anything that has not resolved in 10 days. Do not self-diagnose bone pain on the internet.
- **Cross-train hard** - pool running, cycling, elliptical. You can hold a genuinely large share of your aerobic fitness for weeks this way. Many people have finished marathons after 4-6 weeks of cross-training in place of running.
- Return only when you are pain-free walking, then pain-free running 20 minutes on consecutive days.
- **A deferred marathon is a marathon you will still run. An ignored stress fracture is a year.**

## Race Week

### The Final Seven Days
- **Do not add running.** The taper is the plan, not a lull in it. You cannot gain fitness in a week and you can absolutely lose the race in one.
- **Nothing new.** No new shoes, gels, socks, kit, breakfast, supplements, massage, or stretching routine. Everything on race day should be something you have done at least five times.
- **Carb-load for the final 2-3 days.** Increase carbohydrate to roughly 7-10g per kg of body weight per day, reduce fat and fibre. You are not eating *more food*, you are shifting *what* the food is. Practice a mini version of this before a long run so race week is not a surprise to your gut.
- **Hydrate steadily** across the week. Pale yellow urine. Add a little sodium.
- **Prioritise sleep two nights before.** The night before a marathon is often broken. That is normal, it will not hurt your race, and it is exactly why the night before *that* one matters.
- **Lay everything out the night before.** Bib, pins, shoes, socks, kit, gels, watch (charged), throwaway layer, breakfast, transport plan.

### Race Morning
- Wake at least 3 hours before the gun.
- Eat your rehearsed breakfast 2-3 hours before: 400-600 calories, mostly carbohydrate, low fat and fibre.
- Get to the start early. The toilet queue is always worse than you planned for.
- **Warm up almost not at all.** 5 minutes of easy jogging and a couple of strides. You have 26.2 miles in which to warm up, and every calorie spent before the gun is a calorie you want at mile 22.
- Line up further back than feels right. Everyone lines up too far forward.

## Race Strategy: How to Actually Run 26.2

### The First Marathon Pacing Plan

- **Miles 1-6: SLOWER THAN GOAL PACE. 20-30 seconds per mile slower.** This is the hardest discipline in the sport. You will feel incredible. Hundreds of people will stream past you. Every one of them who is a first-timer going out fast will be walking by mile 21, and you will pass them. **Bank patience, not time.** Time banked in the first 10K is repaid with 300% interest after mile 20.
- **Miles 7-13: Ease into goal effort.** Settle. Relax the shoulders and jaw. Fuel on schedule, not on feel. Run the tangents.
- **Miles 14-19: Hold. Stay boring.** This should feel controlled and slightly dull. If it feels hard here, you went out too fast - slow down NOW, immediately, while it is still a small problem.
- **Miles 20-23: This is the marathon.** Everything before this was just getting to the start line of the real race. It will hurt. That is expected and it does not mean anything is wrong. Shrink your world: get to the next mile marker. Then the next one.
- **Miles 24-26.2: Whatever is left.** If you paced it right, you are passing people and you will remember it for the rest of your life.

**If you are still running at mile 22, you have succeeded.** That is the actual goal of this plan, stated as a checkpoint.

### The Wall
- Usually mile 18-22. It is glycogen depletion, not weakness.
- **You prevent it with three things:** the aerobic base you built over 20 weeks, disciplined early pacing, and fueling that started at 45 minutes rather than at mile 18.
- **If you hit it anyway:** take fuel immediately, slow down deliberately, walk if you need to, and keep moving forward. Walking is not quitting. You will finish.

### If It Goes Wrong
- **Went out too fast, struggling at 18:** Slow down hard and immediately, take fuel and fluid, switch to run/walk. Do not stop. You will finish.
- **Cramping:** Slow down, walk briefly, take fluid and sodium. Do not aggressively stretch a cramping muscle mid-race.
- **GI trouble:** Stop the gels, take water only, slow down. Uncomfortable, not race-ending.
- **Hotter than expected:** Add 30-45 seconds per mile from mile 1 and abandon the time goal entirely. Heat is a physiological tax, not a test of will. Runners who ignore it end up in the medical tent.

For a fuller treatment: [race-day execution](../concepts/race-day-execution.md).

## After the Race

- Keep walking 10-15 minutes. Do not sit straight down.
- Eat and drink within 30-60 minutes: carbohydrate plus protein.
- **Do not run for at least 5-7 days.** None. Walking and gentle swimming only.
- Weeks 2-3: easy running only, short, every other day, and only if you feel like it.
- **No hard workouts for 4 weeks.**
- Traditional guide: one easy day per mile raced, so about 26 days before you train seriously again. It sounds excessive. It is not - the muscle damage and immune suppression from a marathon are real and last longer than the soreness does.
- Expect a mood dip a few days after. Twenty weeks of purpose just ended. It is extremely common, it passes, and picking a modest next goal helps.

## Customization Guidelines

### If You Can Only Run 4 Days a Week
- Drop the Sunday recovery run entirely. Do not redistribute the miles.
- Your peak week becomes about 36-37 miles. **This is entirely sufficient to finish a marathon healthy.** Four honest days beats five where the fifth one breaks you.
- Keep all three long runs. They are the plan.

### If You Are Using Run/Walk
- Follow the plan exactly as written. Mileage is mileage.
- Practice your chosen ratio on **every** long run from Week 1.
- Your long runs will take longer in clock time. Budget for it - a run/walk 20-miler can be 4+ hours. Plan the route, the fuel, and the water accordingly.

### If a Week Feels Too Hard
- Repeat the previous week rather than progressing. There is no penalty. There is no prize for staying on schedule while injured.
- If two consecutive weeks feel too hard, cut all volume 20% for a week and reassess honestly whether your easy runs are actually easy. They usually are not.

### If It All Feels Too Easy
- **Good. That is the design.** Do not add miles. Do not add speedwork. Do not run the long runs faster.
- Surplus capacity goes into [strength work](../fundamentals/strength-training.md), sleep, or cross-training - not into running miles.
- Feeling strong in Week 10 is what a well-executed base phase produces. It is not evidence you should train harder; it is evidence the plan is working.
- If you finish this cycle healthy and hungry, the [intermediate 18-week plan](18-week-marathon-training-plan.md) is your next cycle.

### Strength Training
- 2x per week, 20-30 minutes: squats, lunges, single-leg deadlifts, calf raises, glute bridges, planks.
- Monday and Thursday, after running, never the day before a long run.
- For a first marathon this is one of the single highest-return injury-prevention investments available. See [strength training](../fundamentals/strength-training.md).

## Key Takeaways

1. **Finish healthy. Time goals are for the second marathon.** You cannot know your marathon pace until you have run a marathon. The first one is the measurement, not the exam.
2. **Still running at mile 22 = success.** That is the real target. Everything in this plan is built backwards from it.
3. **Easy means EASY.** Twenty weeks of easy days run slightly too hard is the most reliable way to arrive at the start line already worn down. Slow down.
4. **Long runs are run SLOW - slower than goal pace, not at it.** Their job is time on your feet, not rehearsing race speed. This is counterintuitive and it is correct.
5. **Volume before intensity, never both at once.** Twelve weeks of miles, then a small dose of effort. In that order.
6. **A down week every 4th week is where the fitness actually lands.** Skipping it does not make you fitter.
7. **Fuel on every long run over 90 minutes. Nothing new on race day.** The marathon is a fuel-management problem and your gut needs 14 weeks of training too.
8. **Run/walk is a legitimate strategy, not a fallback.** Decide before the race, practice it in training, and start the breaks at mile 1. Many first marathons are best run this way.
9. **Go out slow.** 20-30 seconds per mile slower than goal pace for the first 6 miles. Bank patience, not time.
10. **Consistency beats heroism.** Twenty weeks of unglamorous, mostly-easy, well-fuelled running is what carries you to the finish. There are no shortcuts and you do not need any.
