# 12-Week 10K Training Plan (Advanced Level)

## Plan Overview

**Target Audience:** Runners with multiple years of consistent training, a racing history at 5K and 10K, and a 40+ mpw base held year-round
**Goal:** A peak 10K performance in 12 weeks
**Weekly Mileage:** 50-75 miles, peaking at 75
**Running Days:** 6-7 per week, with doubles on 1-2 days
**Experience Required:** You already run 40+ miles most weeks without breaking down, you have done structured threshold and interval work, and you know your current race fitness

This is the sibling of `12-week-5k-training-plan-advanced.md`, moved up a distance. The volume is higher, the long run is longer, and - the key structural difference - **the threshold session outranks the interval session.** The 10K is fundamentally a lactate-threshold race run slightly above threshold. VO2max still matters (it sets the ceiling), but the winner at this distance is the runner who can hold the highest fraction of that ceiling for 30-40 minutes.

If you are not currently holding 40+ mpw, use `12-week-10k-training-plan.md` instead.

## Before You Start

1. **Determine Your Current Fitness:**
   - Use a recent race result (5K, 10K, or half) from within the last 6 weeks
   - Convert it to a VDOT value
   - Use the fitness you have, not the fitness you want

2. **Know Your Training Paces by Zone:**
   - **Easy (E):** Conversational. Aerobic development and recovery. The bulk of your mileage
   - **Marathon (M):** Steady, controlled. Useful as a long-run finish and as a bridge between E and T
   - **Threshold (T):** "Comfortably hard," roughly one-hour race pace. **The most important zone in this plan**
   - **Interval (I):** Roughly current 3K-5K race pace. VO2max
   - **Repetition (R):** Roughly current mile race pace. Economy and mechanics, not aerobic stress

   **Convert your VDOT into actual per-mile and per-400m numbers using `../reference/vdot-and-pace-tables.md`.** Every session below names a zone, never a number, on purpose.

3. **Set Up Your Race Calendar:**
   - Goal 10K: Sunday of Week 12
   - Tune-up 5K: Saturday of Week 8 (built into the schedule)

4. **Know Your Goal 10K Pace:**
   For an advanced runner, goal 10K pace sits just faster than threshold pace and well slower than interval pace. You will train at it directly in Weeks 9 and 10. Get the number from your VDOT table, not from ambition.

## Training Philosophy

- **Weeks 1-4 (Base + Threshold):** Volume climbs, threshold carries the quality load, hill sprints and strides maintain the neuromuscular system
- **Weeks 5-8 (Build):** Two quality sessions per week. VO2max intervals enter alongside heavier threshold work. Volume peaks in Week 7. Week 8 is a down week with a tune-up 5K
- **Weeks 9-11 (Peak and Sharpen):** Week 9 is the last big week and the first true race-pace work. Weeks 10-11 drop volume while holding intensity, and specificity rises
- **Week 12 (Taper and Race):** Volume falls hard, quality goes short and sharp

### The Structure of a Hard Week

Three stress days, every week, always: **an interval session (Tuesday), a threshold session (Saturday), and a long run (Sunday).** Everything else is easy or recovery. Nothing else.

At 65-75 miles per week you have roughly four easy days carrying 35-40 miles. Those runs exist to be aerobic and nothing else. **The failure mode at this volume is not that the hard days are too hard - it is that the easy days drift.** A five-mile "easy" run at 30 seconds per mile too fast, four times a week, is an unplanned quality session you never budgeted for, and it will show up as a slow Tuesday and a flat race.

Protect the easy days like they are workouts. They are.

### Why the Threshold Session Is the Anchor

A 10K is raced at roughly 95-100% of VO2max-limited effort but sustained for 30-40 minutes - which means it is fought at and just above lactate threshold for the entire duration. Raise the threshold and the whole race gets easier. That is why this plan carries more threshold volume than its 5K sibling, why the long run picks up marathon-pace work, and why the peak Saturday sessions get long.

VO2max intervals are still in the plan (you cannot race a fast 10K with a low ceiling), but they are the supporting act.

## Doubles: When They Help and When They Are Junk

Doubles appear on Wednesday, and in the biggest weeks they are what makes 70-75 mpw achievable without adding a fourth stress day.

**A second run helps when:**
- It adds aerobic volume you could not absorb in a single run without turning that run into a stress
- It is genuinely easy or recovery pace, and it flushes the legs rather than fatiguing them
- It is separated from the AM run by at least 6 hours
- You are already at 6 running days and the alternative is a single run long enough to leave a mark

**A second run is junk mileage when:**
- It is a short shuffle bolted on to make the weekly number look right
- It is run at the same effort as the morning run, converting easy volume into moderate volume
- It lands on a day that already had quality and it eats into tomorrow
- Dropping it would not change a single stress in your week - and you cannot say why you are doing it

**Rule of thumb:** the PM leg of a double should be the slowest run in your week. If it is not, it is not doing the job you think it is doing.

## The 12-Week Plan

### Phase 1: Base + Threshold (Weeks 1-4)

#### Week 1: Establish the Load (55 miles)
- **Monday:** Recovery 5 miles
- **Tuesday:** **Threshold** - 2mi warmup + 25 min continuous at T + 2mi cooldown = 9 miles
- **Wednesday:** **Double** - AM easy 6 miles, PM easy 4 miles = 10 miles
- **Thursday:** Easy 8 miles + 8x100m strides
- **Friday:** Recovery 5 miles
- **Saturday:** Easy 6 miles with 8x10-second hill sprints (full walk-down recovery)
- **Sunday:** Long run 12 miles easy

#### Week 2: Build Volume (60 miles)
- **Monday:** Recovery 5 miles
- **Tuesday:** **Threshold (cruise intervals)** - 2mi warmup + 5x1 mile at T w/60sec jog + 2mi cooldown = 9 miles
- **Wednesday:** **Double** - AM easy 7 miles, PM easy 4 miles = 11 miles
- **Thursday:** Easy 9 miles + 8x100m strides
- **Friday:** Recovery 5 miles
- **Saturday:** Easy 7 miles with 10x10-second hill sprints
- **Sunday:** Long run 14 miles easy

#### Week 3: Peak Base Volume (65 miles)
- **Monday:** Recovery 6 miles
- **Tuesday:** **Threshold** - 2mi warmup + 30 min continuous at T + 2mi cooldown = 10 miles
- **Wednesday:** **Double** - AM easy 7 miles, PM easy 5 miles = 12 miles
- **Thursday:** Easy 9 miles + 8x100m strides
- **Friday:** Recovery 5 miles
- **Saturday:** **Repetition** - 2mi warmup + 10x200m at R w/200m jog + 2mi cooldown = 7 miles
- **Sunday:** Long run 16 miles easy (last 3 at M pace if feeling strong)

#### Week 4: Recovery Week (50 miles)
- **Monday:** Recovery 5 miles
- **Tuesday:** Easy 9 miles + 8x100m strides
- **Wednesday:** Easy 8 miles (single run, no double this week)
- **Thursday:** **Light Threshold** - 2mi warmup + 3x1 mile at T w/60sec jog + 2mi cooldown = 7 miles
- **Friday:** Recovery 4 miles
- **Saturday:** Easy 6 miles + 6x100m strides
- **Sunday:** Long run 11 miles easy

> **Take the down week.** See "The Cost of a Missed Recovery Week" below. It is the week that turns Weeks 1-3 into fitness rather than fatigue.

---

### Phase 2: Build - VO2max Enters (Weeks 5-8)

#### Week 5: Introduce Intervals (65 miles)
- **Monday:** Recovery 5 miles
- **Tuesday:** **Intervals (VO2max)** - 3mi warmup + 5x1000m at I w/3min jog recovery + 3mi cooldown = 10 miles
- **Wednesday:** **Double** - AM easy 7 miles, PM easy 5 miles = 12 miles
- **Thursday:** Easy 9 miles + 8x100m strides
- **Friday:** Recovery 5 miles
- **Saturday:** **Threshold** - 3mi warmup + 2x(15 min at T) w/3min jog between + 3mi cooldown = 11 miles
- **Sunday:** Long run 13 miles easy

#### Week 6: Build the Threshold (70 miles)
- **Monday:** Recovery 6 miles
- **Tuesday:** **Intervals (VO2max)** - 3mi warmup + 6x800m at I w/2:30 jog recovery + 3mi cooldown = 10 miles
- **Wednesday:** **Double** - AM easy 8 miles, PM easy 5 miles = 13 miles
- **Thursday:** Easy 10 miles + 8x100m strides
- **Friday:** Recovery 5 miles
- **Saturday:** **Threshold (cruise intervals)** - 3mi warmup + 6x1 mile at T w/60sec jog + 3mi cooldown = 12 miles
- **Sunday:** Long run 14 miles easy (last 4 at M pace)

#### Week 7: Peak Volume (75 miles)
- **Monday:** Recovery 6 miles
- **Tuesday:** **Intervals (VO2max)** - 3mi warmup + 5x1200m at I w/3min jog recovery + 3mi cooldown = 11 miles
- **Wednesday:** **Double** - AM easy 9 miles, PM easy 5 miles = 14 miles
- **Thursday:** Easy 10 miles + 8x100m strides
- **Friday:** Recovery 6 miles
- **Saturday:** **Threshold** - 3mi warmup + 2x(20 min at T) w/4min jog between + 3mi cooldown = 13 miles
- **Sunday:** Long run 15 miles easy

> The biggest week of the plan, and the biggest threshold session. Forty minutes at T is a lot of honest work. If you are going to break, this is where it happens - read the effort, not the schedule.

#### Week 8: Recovery + Tune-Up Race (56 miles)
- **Monday:** Recovery 5 miles
- **Tuesday:** Easy 9 miles + 8x100m strides
- **Wednesday:** Easy 8 miles (single run)
- **Thursday:** **Pre-race sharpener** - 2mi warmup + 6x200m at R w/200m jog + 2mi cooldown = 6 miles
- **Friday:** Recovery 4 miles
- **Saturday:** **TUNE-UP RACE (5K)** - 3mi warmup + 5K race + 3mi cooldown = 9 miles
- **Sunday:** Long run 15 miles easy

**On the tune-up race:** race it properly. A 5K four weeks out gives you (a) a hard, current fitness number to re-derive your VDOT and therefore your goal 10K pace from, (b) a rehearsal of the warm-up, the start, and the first-mile discipline, and (c) four weeks of runway to fix whatever it exposes. A 5K raced flat out is a very reliable predictor of what you can hold for 10K. Update your paces in `../reference/vdot-and-pace-tables.md` before the peak block.

---

### Phase 3: Peak and Sharpen (Weeks 9-11)

The last three weeks before race week are where advanced self-coached runners most often go wrong, in one of two ways: they keep stacking volume to the end and arrive fit but flat, or they cut everything and arrive fresh but blunt. The correct move is narrow.

**Volume comes down. Intensity stays exactly where it was. Specificity rises.** Every week from here is smaller than the last, and every week the quality sits closer to goal 10K pace.

#### Week 9: Final Big Week (70 miles)
- **Monday:** Recovery 6 miles
- **Tuesday:** **Intervals (VO2max)** - 3mi warmup + 6x1000m at I w/2:30 jog recovery + 3mi cooldown = 11 miles
- **Wednesday:** **Double** - AM easy 8 miles, PM easy 5 miles = 13 miles
- **Thursday:** Easy 10 miles + 8x100m strides
- **Friday:** Recovery 5 miles
- **Saturday:** **Threshold + Race Pace** - 3mi warmup + 4x1 mile at T w/60sec jog + 4min rest + 3x1000m at goal 10K pace w/2min jog + 3mi cooldown = 12 miles
- **Sunday:** Long run 13 miles easy

#### Week 10: Sharpening Begins (63 miles)
- **Monday:** Recovery 5 miles
- **Tuesday:** **Intervals (VO2max)** - 3mi warmup + 5x1000m at I w/3min jog recovery + 3mi cooldown = 10 miles
- **Wednesday:** **Double** - AM easy 7 miles, PM easy 4 miles = 11 miles
- **Thursday:** Easy 9 miles + 8x100m strides
- **Friday:** Recovery 5 miles
- **Saturday:** **Race-Pace Specific** - 3mi warmup + 5 miles continuous at goal 10K pace + 3mi cooldown = 11 miles
- **Sunday:** Long run 12 miles easy

> The key session of the plan. Five continuous miles at goal 10K pace, two weeks out, is the closest thing to a dress rehearsal you get. It should feel hard and controlled - if it feels desperate, your goal pace is wrong and you now know it in time to change it.

#### Week 11: Sharp and Light (54 miles)
- **Monday:** Recovery 5 miles
- **Tuesday:** **Intervals (VO2max)** - 2mi warmup + 12x400m at I w/400m jog recovery + 2mi cooldown = 10 miles
- **Wednesday:** **Double** - AM easy 5 miles, PM easy 4 miles = 9 miles
- **Thursday:** Easy 8 miles + 8x100m strides
- **Friday:** Recovery 4 miles
- **Saturday:** **Threshold + Repetition** - 2mi warmup + 3x1 mile at T w/60sec jog + 4x200m at R w/200m jog + 2mi cooldown = 8 miles
- **Sunday:** Long run 10 miles easy

---

### Phase 4: Taper + Race (Week 12)

#### Week 12: Race Week (36 miles including race)
- **Monday:** Recovery 4 miles
- **Tuesday:** **Sharpener** - 2mi warmup + 2 miles at T + 3min rest + 4x400m at I w/400m jog + 2mi cooldown = 8 miles
- **Wednesday:** Easy 5 miles + 6x100m strides
- **Thursday:** Easy 5 miles with 4x200m at R (relaxed, not straining)
- **Friday:** Rest
- **Saturday:** Shakeout 3 miles easy + 4x100m strides
- **Sunday:** **RACE DAY - 10K Goal Race** - 3mi warmup + 10K race + 2mi cooldown = 11 miles

**Taper logic:** volume falls from 54 to 36 (about a third), but Tuesday still touches T and I pace, and Thursday still touches R. You are shedding fatigue, not fitness. Ten days of pure easy running before a 10K will leave you rested and dull - the short, sharp touches are what keep the edge on.

## Weekly Mileage Progression

```
Week 1:  55 miles (base + threshold)
Week 2:  60 miles (build)
Week 3:  65 miles (peak base)
Week 4:  50 miles (RECOVERY)
Week 5:  65 miles (intervals enter)
Week 6:  70 miles (build threshold)
Week 7:  75 miles (PEAK VOLUME)
Week 8:  56 miles (RECOVERY + tune-up 5K)
Week 9:  70 miles (final big week, race pace enters)
Week 10: 63 miles (sharpen: volume down, intensity held)
Week 11: 54 miles (sharpen)
Week 12: 36 miles (taper + RACE)
```

Down weeks in Week 4 and Week 8. Weeks 10-12 are the sharpening descent. No week jumps more than 5-6 miles from the previous non-recovery week.

## Workout Descriptions

### Easy Runs (E)
- **Effort:** Conversational, full sentences. Zone 2
- **Purpose:** Aerobic development - capillary density, mitochondrial volume, stroke volume - plus recovery from the three stress days
- **Share of the plan:** Roughly 80% of total mileage
- **The rule:** If you cannot talk, it is not easy. At 70+ mpw, easy-day pace creep is the most reliable way to ruin a good block

### Recovery Runs
- **Effort:** Slower than easy, deliberately
- **Purpose:** Blood flow and free aerobic volume
- **Placement:** Day after quality, and as the PM leg of doubles
- **Note:** A recovery run cannot be too slow. It can very easily be too fast

### Threshold (T) - The Anchor
- **Effort:** Comfortably hard, roughly one-hour race pace. Three-to-five word answers
- **Purpose:** Raise lactate threshold. **For a 10K this is the highest-leverage adaptation available to you**
- **Two forms in this plan, and they do different jobs:**
  - **Continuous tempo:** one unbroken block (25-30 min). Trains the metabolic *and* psychological capacity to hold a steady, uncomfortable pace with zero relief - which is exactly what the middle of a 10K is
  - **Cruise intervals:** mile reps at T with 60 seconds of jog between. Same time-at-threshold, less accumulated fatigue, and much easier to hold the correct pace on every rep
- **Choosing between them:**
  - Use **continuous** when the total threshold volume is moderate (20-30 min) and the goal is rehearsing sustained discomfort. For a 10K runner, some continuous tempo is non-negotiable - the race does not give you 60-second jogs
  - Use **cruise intervals** when you want *more total time at true T pace than you could hold continuously* (35-45 min), when you are on tired legs, when the weather is against you, or when you have a history of drifting off pace in the back half of continuous tempos. The short recovery exists to keep the pace honest, not to make the session easy
  - **Week 7's 2x20 min with a 4-min jog** is the hybrid: long enough blocks to be genuinely continuous in feel, split just enough to let you hold true T pace for 40 total minutes
- **Warning:** If you finish a tempo wrecked, you ran it at I pace. You did not get a threshold workout, you got a bad interval workout

### Intervals (I) - VO2max
- **Effort:** Roughly current 3K-5K race pace. Zone 5
- **Purpose:** Raise VO2max, which sets the ceiling that your threshold is a fraction of
- **Rep length:** 800m-1200m is the sweet spot - long enough to actually reach VO2max within the rep, short enough to accumulate volume at the correct pace
- **Recovery:** Jog recoveries of roughly 50-90% of the rep duration, as prescribed. The recovery is a tool, not a weakness. Cutting it does not make the workout better, it makes the later reps slower, which makes the workout worse
- **Total I volume:** Cap it around 8% of weekly mileage. More VO2max work is not more VO2max
- **The rule:** Every rep at the same pace, and rep 6 should be no slower than rep 1. If it is, you started too fast

### Repetition (R)
- **Effort:** Roughly current mile race pace. Fast, relaxed, mechanically clean
- **Purpose:** Running economy and neuromuscular recruitment. **Not an aerobic workout.** You are not trying to get out of breath
- **Recovery:** Full or near-full. Recover until you can hit the next rep with identical quality
- **Why a 10K runner needs it:** Economy is a separate lever from VO2max and threshold. Two runners with the same VDOT can differ significantly over 10K purely on oxygen cost per mile. R work attacks that directly, and it costs almost nothing aerobically
- **The rule:** If you are gasping, you turned R work into I work and got neither adaptation

### Marathon Pace (M)
- **Effort:** Steady, controlled, clearly harder than easy but well short of threshold
- **Purpose:** Aerobic-strength work and a bridge between easy volume and threshold. Used here only as a long-run finish in the build phase
- **Note:** Do not let M-pace finishes turn into T-pace finishes. That converts your long run into a fourth stress day, and the schedule has no room for one

### Strides
- **Distance:** 100m, accelerating to roughly mile race pace, holding briefly, decelerating. Full walk-back recovery
- **Purpose:** Neuromuscular maintenance at essentially zero physiological cost
- **Frequency:** 2-3x per week after easy runs
- **Why they are not optional:** In a 70 mpw block, a stride is what stops your mechanics from slowly collapsing into a heavy, low-cadence aerobic shuffle by Week 8. Skip them and you will arrive on race day aerobically excellent and mechanically flat

### Hill Sprints
- **Format:** 8-10 seconds, near-maximal, up a steep hill, full walk-down recovery (2-3 min)
- **Purpose:** Maximal fiber recruitment and tendon/connective-tissue strengthening with minimal impact stress, because you are moving slowly in absolute terms
- **Placement:** After an easy run, base phase only (Weeks 1-2)
- **Note:** Never a workout. If your lungs are burning, you did too many

### Long Run
- **Pace:** Easy, with optional M-pace finishes in the build phase
- **Length:** Roughly 20-25% of weekly mileage, capped around 16 miles. Longer than the 5K plan's, because 10K performance is more aerobically constrained
- **Note:** Two quality days already exist in your week. Resist the urge to make the long run a third

## The Risk at This Volume

Seventy-five miles a week with two quality sessions is enough training to change what you are capable of over 10K. It is also enough to injure you, or to dig a hole you cannot get out of before race day. The margin between those two outcomes is recovery, and recovery is the first thing people cut, because cutting it feels like nothing.

### The Cost of a Missed Recovery Week

Skipping Week 4 or Week 8 is the most expensive decision available to you here, and it never feels expensive in the moment. You are fit, you are rolling, and giving back 20 miles feels like a waste.

What you actually gave up:

- **Adaptation happens in the down week, not the build week.** Weeks 1-3 create the stimulus; Week 4 is when your body cashes it. Skip Week 4 and you have paid for three weeks of training and collected nothing
- **Fatigue accumulates silently.** Connective-tissue microdamage, glycogen depletion, and central fatigue do not report themselves week to week. They report all at once, usually about 10 days later, usually as an injury or a sudden inexplicable flatline
- **The damage compounds asymmetrically.** A skipped recovery week does not cost you one week. It costs the recovery week plus the 2-4 weeks of degraded training that follow, because you start Week 5 in deficit and go deeper every week after
- **You cannot make it up.** A tibial stress reaction in Week 9 is not repairable by Week 12. The race is gone

Treat a recovery week the way you would treat your peak threshold session: as a required, load-bearing part of the plan.

### Overtraining Warning Signs

Any single one is noise. Two or more, sustained across a week, means back off now:

- **Elevated resting heart rate** (5+ bpm over your morning baseline for several consecutive days)
- **Suppressed heart rate at effort** - you cannot drive HR up in a workout and the pace feels hard anyway. This is the more serious signal
- **Sleep disturbance** despite heavy load. Waking at 3am, or sleeping long and waking unrefreshed
- **Persistent soreness** that does not clear inside 48 hours
- **Appetite loss** or unintended weight drop
- **Mood shift** - irritability, apathy, dread before sessions you used to enjoy
- **Recurring minor illness** - the cold that keeps coming back
- **Paces slipping** at equal or higher perceived effort, across more than one session
- **Any localized pain that alters your gait.** That is not fatigue. That is an injury forming. Stop today

**The correct response is always smaller than you fear:** 3-7 days easy or off, then restart at the previous week's load. You will lose almost nothing. Training through it costs the block.

For the physiology, read `../fundamentals/recovery.md`. For the injuries this volume produces and how to catch them early, read `../fundamentals/injury-prevention.md`.

### The Failure Mode Nobody Admits To

It is not the Tuesday intervals. It is Wednesday.

At 70 mpw with three stress days, roughly 40 of those miles are easy or recovery. If those runs creep upward toward "moderate but honestly it felt fine," you have quietly converted a polarized week into a grey week. You will feel strong for about 10 days. Then the intervals start slipping, the Saturday tempo starts feeling like a race, your resting HR ticks up, and you will conclude that you need to work harder.

You do not. You need Wednesday to be slow.

## Customization Guidelines

### If You Need to Miss a Workout
1. **Missed easy or recovery run:** Skip it. It is aerobic volume; one run is statistical noise
2. **Missed long run:** Move it, do not delete it. Push it to Monday and drop Monday's recovery run
3. **Missed threshold session:** This is the one to reschedule if you reschedule anything. It is the most important workout in a 10K build. Move it a day if you can still keep 48 hours between stress days
4. **Missed interval session:** Drop it if it cannot be moved cleanly. **Never** stack two quality sessions on consecutive days to catch up
5. **Missed more than 4 days:** Do not resume where you left off. Repeat the previous week and rebuild

### If You Cannot Run 6-7 Days
Cut the PM leg of the double first, then the Monday recovery run. Never cut a quality session or the long run to protect an easy day. This plan works at 58-60 mpw on 6 days as long as the three stress days stay intact.

### If Workouts Feel Too Hard
In descending order of likelihood:
1. **Your easy days are too fast.** This is the answer most of the time
2. **You are training off goal paces instead of current paces.** Re-derive VDOT from your last real race
3. **You are under-fueling or under-sleeping.** Seventy miles a week has a real caloric and sleep cost, and neither is optional
4. **You skipped or shortened a down week**
5. **You are genuinely overtrained.** See the warning-sign list

### If Workouts Feel Too Easy
1. **Check your paces first.** Easy-feeling quality usually means a stale or soft VDOT input. Race the Week 8 tune-up and re-derive
2. **Add aerobic volume before intensity.** Extend the PM leg of the double, or add a mile to the long run
3. Do not add a third quality day, and do not push I-pace volume past ~8% of the week. At this level, more easy volume and a higher threshold is the road forward, not more red-line work

### Adjusting for a Different Race Day
The plan assumes a Sunday race. For a Saturday race, shift the whole week back a day (quality Monday and Friday, long run Saturday), preserving the 48-hour spacing between stress days.

## Race Week and 10K Racing Strategy

### Days Out
- **6 days out (Tuesday):** Last real workout. Short, sharp, touching T and I. Stimulate, do not stress
- **4-5 days out:** Easy running with strides
- **3 days out:** Easy with a few relaxed R-pace touches
- **2 days out:** Rest
- **1 day out:** Short shakeout with strides

### Race Day Warm-Up
- 15-20 min easy jogging
- Dynamic drills
- 4-6x100m strides, building into race pace
- Finish 5-8 minutes before the gun

### Race Pacing
The 10K punishes an aggressive first mile more than almost any other distance, because you are already above threshold and lactate accumulates from the gun.

- **Mile 1:** Deliberately controlled. Target goal pace or 2-3 seconds slower. Every second you bank here you repay with interest in mile 5
- **Miles 2-4:** Lock onto goal pace. This is the grind. Latch onto a group, hold the rhythm, stop doing arithmetic
- **Mile 5:** The mile that decides the race and the mile everyone wants to concede. Do not concede it. Hold form, raise cadence, stay on the runner ahead
- **Mile 6 and the last 0.2:** Progressive to all-out. If you paced correctly, you have something and it will hurt to spend it. Spend it

### After the Race
- Cool-down: 15-20 min easy
- 3-5 days of easy running or complete rest
- No quality for at least a week
- Racing again in 2-4 weeks? Hold your easy volume and run one sharpening session per week. Otherwise take 1-2 weeks unstructured before the next block

## Key Takeaways

1. **The 10K is a threshold race.** The Saturday threshold session is the most important workout in this plan. If something has to go, it is not that one
2. **Two quality sessions plus a long run - and nothing else is hard.** Three stress days, four easy days. That separation is the plan
3. **The easy days carry the load.** At 65-75 mpw, easy-pace creep is the single most reliable way to blow up an advanced block
4. **Doubles buy aerobic volume and nothing else.** Run the PM leg slower than any other run in your week, or do not run it at all
5. **Continuous tempo rehearses sustained discomfort; cruise intervals accumulate more time at true threshold pace.** Use both, deliberately, for different reasons
6. **Intervals raise the ceiling, threshold raises the fraction of the ceiling you can hold, repetitions make you cheaper to run.** Three distinct adaptations, three distinct paces - get them from `../reference/vdot-and-pace-tables.md`
7. **Strides and hill sprints are free.** They keep a high-volume runner from turning into a shuffler
8. **Race the Week 8 tune-up 5K for real.** It gives you a true goal 10K pace with four weeks left to act on it
9. **The last three weeks: volume down, intensity held, specificity up.** Tapering is not detraining
10. **The recovery weeks are load-bearing.** Skipping one does not save a week, it costs three - and sometimes it costs the race
