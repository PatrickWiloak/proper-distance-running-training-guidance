# 16-Week Half Marathon Training Plan (Beginner Level)

## Plan Overview

**Target Audience:** First-time half marathoners. You have run a 5K or 10K, you are comfortable covering 4-5 miles, and you have been running consistently for at least 2-3 months.
**Goal:** Finish the half marathon (13.1 miles) strong and healthy in 16 weeks
**Weekly Mileage:** 19-33 miles (peak 33)
**Running Days:** 4-5 per week
**Peak Long Run:** 12 miles
**Experience Required:** Able to run 40-45 minutes continuously without walking. That is it.

This is the sibling plan to the [16-week intermediate half marathon plan](16-week-half-marathon-training-plan.md). If you already train 30-35 miles per week and have raced this distance, go there instead. If this is your first half, start here.

## Before You Start

1. **Set the Right Goal:**
   - Your goal for a first half marathon is to **finish strong and healthy**.
   - Time goals are for your second one. Really.
   - "Strong" means you are still running well at mile 11, not surviving it.

2. **Establish Your Baseline:**
   - You should be running 3-4 days per week already, roughly 12-18 miles per week.
   - You should have covered 4-5 miles in one run at least once in the past month.
   - If you cannot do this yet, spend 4-6 weeks building to it before starting Week 1. Starting this plan under-prepared is the single most common route to a stress fracture.

3. **Know Your Two Paces (that is all you need):**
   - **Easy:** Conversational. You can speak in full sentences. Zone 2, 60-70% max HR.
   - **Steady:** Slightly firmer but still controlled. Zone 3, 70-80% max HR. Used sparingly.
   - You do not need interval pace, VO2max pace, or a VDOT score for this plan. You need aerobic fitness and time on your feet.
   - See [heart-rate zones](../concepts/heart-rate-zones.md) and the [HR zones table](../visuals/heart-rate-zones-table.md) if you want the detail.

4. **Schedule Your Race:**
   - Pick a half marathon 16 weeks out and register. A paid entry is a commitment device.
   - Optional: a 5K or 10K tune-up race in Week 12 to practice race-day logistics.

5. **Get Your Shoes Sorted:**
   - One pair of well-fitting running shoes with fewer than 300 miles on them.
   - Rotating two pairs is better if you can afford it.

## Training Philosophy

The half marathon is an aerobic event. For a first-timer, essentially 100% of the improvement you need comes from consistent easy aerobic running and a steadily growing long run. Not from speedwork.

**The four principles this plan is built on:**

1. **Easy means EASY.** This is the principle beginners break most often. If you cannot hold a conversation, you are running too fast. Most first-timers run their easy days at a moderate, mildly uncomfortable pace, accumulate fatigue for 16 weeks, and arrive at the start line already worn down. Slow down. See [the easy/hard rule](../concepts/easy-hard.md).

2. **Long runs are run SLOW - slower than goal race pace, not at it.** This feels wrong. If you want to race 13.1 at 10:00/mile, your long run should be at 10:45-11:30/mile. The long run's job is to spend time on your feet and stimulate aerobic adaptations - mitochondria, capillaries, fat oxidation. Those adaptations come from *duration*, not intensity. Running long runs at goal pace makes them a race, wrecks the following week, and teaches you nothing you need. See [the aerobic system](../fundamentals/aerobic-system.md).

3. **Build volume before intensity, and never raise both in the same week.** This plan builds mileage first (Weeks 1-8), then adds a little controlled steady effort (Weeks 9-15). It never asks you to run more miles *and* harder miles in the same week.

4. **Every 4th week is a down week.** Weeks 4, 8, and 12 cut volume by 20-25%. Adaptation happens during recovery, not during the hard work. The down week is not optional and it is not a sign of weakness. See [recovery](../fundamentals/recovery.md).

**Training Phases:**
- **Weeks 1-4:** Foundation (get consistent, get comfortable)
- **Weeks 5-8:** Base building (grow the long run)
- **Weeks 9-12:** Endurance development (long run reaches 11 miles, introduce steady effort)
- **Weeks 13-15:** Peak and early taper (longest run, then cut back)
- **Week 16:** Race week

## The 16-Week Plan

**Weekly template:**
- **Monday:** Rest or cross-train (bike, swim, walk - 30-40 min easy)
- **Tuesday:** Easy run
- **Wednesday:** Easy run
- **Thursday:** Easy run
- **Friday:** Rest
- **Saturday:** Long run (slow)
- **Sunday:** Recovery run (very easy) or rest

Shift days to fit your life. Just keep one rest day before the long run and never stack two demanding days back to back.

- **Monday:** Rest or cross-train
- **Tuesday:** Easy 4 miles
- **Wednesday:** Easy 3 miles
- **Thursday:** Easy 4 miles
- **Friday:** Rest
- **Saturday:** Long run 5 miles easy
- **Sunday:** Recovery 3 miles very easy

#### Week 2: Settle In (20 miles)
- **Monday:** Rest or cross-train
- **Tuesday:** Easy 4 miles
- **Wednesday:** Easy 4 miles
- **Thursday:** Easy 3 miles
- **Friday:** Rest
- **Saturday:** Long run 6 miles easy
- **Sunday:** Recovery 3 miles very easy

#### Week 3: First Build (22 miles)
- **Monday:** Rest or cross-train
- **Tuesday:** Easy 4 miles
- **Wednesday:** Easy 4 miles + 4x100m strides
- **Thursday:** Easy 4 miles
- **Friday:** Rest
- **Saturday:** Long run 7 miles easy
- **Sunday:** Recovery 3 miles very easy

#### Week 4: Down Week (17 miles)
- **Monday:** Rest
- **Tuesday:** Easy 3 miles
- **Wednesday:** Easy 4 miles
- **Thursday:** Easy 3 miles
- **Friday:** Rest
- **Saturday:** Long run 5 miles easy
- **Sunday:** Recovery 2 miles very easy

### Phase 2: Base Building (Weeks 5-8)

#### Week 5: Grow the Long Run (23 miles)
- **Monday:** Rest or cross-train
- **Tuesday:** Easy 4 miles
- **Wednesday:** Easy 4 miles + 4x100m strides
- **Thursday:** Easy 4 miles
- **Friday:** Rest
- **Saturday:** Long run 8 miles easy
- **Sunday:** Recovery 3 miles very easy

#### Week 6: Longer Still (25 miles)
- **Monday:** Rest or cross-train
- **Tuesday:** Easy 5 miles
- **Wednesday:** Easy 4 miles + 6x100m strides
- **Thursday:** Easy 4 miles
- **Friday:** Rest
- **Saturday:** Long run 9 miles easy (**start practicing fueling - see below**)
- **Sunday:** Recovery 3 miles very easy

#### Week 7: Double Digits (27 miles)
- **Monday:** Rest or cross-train
- **Tuesday:** Easy 5 miles
- **Wednesday:** Easy 5 miles + 6x100m strides
- **Thursday:** Easy 4 miles
- **Friday:** Rest
- **Saturday:** Long run 10 miles easy (fuel at 45 min, then every 30-40 min)
- **Sunday:** Recovery 3 miles very easy

#### Week 8: Down Week (20 miles)
- **Monday:** Rest
- **Tuesday:** Easy 4 miles
- **Wednesday:** Easy 4 miles + 4x100m strides
- **Thursday:** Easy 3 miles
- **Friday:** Rest
- **Saturday:** Long run 6 miles easy
- **Sunday:** Recovery 3 miles very easy

### Phase 3: Endurance Development (Weeks 9-12)

Steady-effort work appears here for the first time. It is a small dose. If a steady segment leaves you gasping, you are running it too hard - it should feel *controlled*, like an effort you could hold for an hour.

#### Week 9: Introduce Steady Effort (29 miles)
- **Monday:** Rest or cross-train
- **Tuesday:** Easy 6 miles
- **Wednesday:** **Steady** - 1mi easy warmup + 3mi at steady effort + 1mi easy cooldown = 5 miles
- **Thursday:** Easy 5 miles
- **Friday:** Rest
- **Saturday:** Long run 10 miles easy
- **Sunday:** Recovery 3 miles very easy

#### Week 10: Build Both Ends (31 miles)
- **Monday:** Rest or cross-train
- **Tuesday:** Easy 6 miles
- **Wednesday:** **Steady** - 1mi easy warmup + 3mi at steady effort + 1mi easy cooldown = 5 miles
- **Thursday:** Easy 6 miles
- **Friday:** Rest
- **Saturday:** Long run 11 miles easy (full fueling rehearsal)
- **Sunday:** Recovery 3 miles very easy

#### Week 11: Longest So Far (33 miles)
- **Monday:** Rest or cross-train
- **Tuesday:** Easy 7 miles
- **Wednesday:** **Steady** - 1mi easy warmup + 4mi at steady effort + 1mi easy cooldown = 6 miles
- **Thursday:** Easy 5 miles
- **Friday:** Rest
- **Saturday:** Long run 11 miles (first 9 easy, last 2 at goal race pace to see how it feels)
- **Sunday:** Recovery 4 miles very easy

#### Week 12: Down Week (25 miles)
- **Monday:** Rest
- **Tuesday:** Easy 5 miles
- **Wednesday:** Easy 4 miles + 6x100m strides
- **Thursday:** Easy 4 miles
- **Friday:** Rest
- **Saturday:** **OPTIONAL: 5K or 10K tune-up race** OR long run 8 miles easy
- **Sunday:** Recovery 4 miles very easy

*If you race: warm up 1-2 miles, race, cool down 1-2 miles. Total roughly the same. A tune-up race is a rehearsal for race-morning logistics (pinning a bib, the toilet queue, the start-line adrenaline), not a fitness test.*

---

### Phase 4: Peak and Taper (Weeks 13-15)

#### Week 13: Peak Week (33 miles)
- **Monday:** Rest or cross-train
- **Tuesday:** Easy 7 miles
- **Wednesday:** **Steady** - 1mi easy warmup + 3mi at steady effort + 1mi easy cooldown = 5 miles
- **Thursday:** Easy 6 miles
- **Friday:** Rest
- **Saturday:** **PEAK LONG RUN - 12 miles** (first 10 easy, last 2 at goal race pace). Full dress rehearsal: race shoes, race kit, race breakfast, race fueling.
- **Sunday:** Recovery 3 miles very easy

**This is the biggest day of the plan. If you can run 12 miles comfortably, the race is yours - the crowd, the taper, and the adrenaline are worth the last 1.1 miles.**

#### Week 14: Taper Begins (26 miles)
- **Monday:** Rest or cross-train
- **Tuesday:** Easy 5 miles
- **Wednesday:** **Race-pace practice** - 1mi easy + 3mi at goal race pace + 1mi easy = 5 miles
- **Thursday:** Easy 4 miles
- **Friday:** Rest
- **Saturday:** Long run 9 miles easy
- **Sunday:** Recovery 3 miles very easy

#### Week 15: Taper Continues (20 miles)
- **Monday:** Rest
- **Tuesday:** Easy 5 miles + 6x100m strides
- **Wednesday:** Easy 4 miles
- **Thursday:** Easy 4 miles
- **Friday:** Rest
- **Saturday:** Long run 7 miles easy (last long run - resist the urge to add miles)
- **Sunday:** Rest

**You will feel restless and possibly a bit sluggish this week. That is the taper working, not fitness disappearing. Fitness is not lost in 14 days. Fatigue is.**

---

### Phase 5: Race Week (Week 16)

#### Week 16: Race Week (12 miles + race)
- **Monday:** Easy 3 miles
- **Tuesday:** Easy 4 miles + 6x100m strides
- **Wednesday:** Easy 3 miles
- **Thursday:** Rest
- **Friday:** Rest
- **Saturday:** Easy 2 miles with 4x100m strides (shake out the travel legs), OR rest
- **Sunday:** **RACE DAY - HALF MARATHON (13.1 miles)**

## Weekly Mileage Progression

```
Week 1:  19 miles
Week 2:  20 miles
Week 3:  22 miles
Week 4:  17 miles (down week)
Week 5:  23 miles
Week 6:  25 miles
Week 7:  27 miles
Week 8:  20 miles (down week)
Week 9:  29 miles
Week 10: 31 miles
Week 11: 33 miles
Week 12: 25 miles (down week / tune-up race)
Week 13: 33 miles (PEAK)
Week 14: 26 miles (taper, -21%)
Week 15: 20 miles (taper, -23%)
Week 16: 12 miles + RACE
```

No week rises more than about 10% over the previous comparable week. Down weeks cut 20-25%. The taper cuts roughly 20-25% per week across the final three weeks. This is deliberate and it is the part of the plan most people wreck by "feeling good" and adding miles.

## Long Run Progression

```
Week 1:  5 miles
Week 2:  6 miles
Week 3:  7 miles
Week 4:  5 miles (down week)
Week 5:  8 miles
Week 6:  9 miles   <- fueling practice starts here
Week 7:  10 miles
Week 8:  6 miles (down week)
Week 9:  10 miles
Week 10: 11 miles
Week 11: 11 miles (last 2 at race pace)
Week 12: 8 miles or tune-up race
Week 13: 12 miles (last 2 at race pace) <- PEAK
Week 14: 9 miles (taper)
Week 15: 7 miles (taper)
Week 16: RACE (13.1 miles)
```

**Why you never run 13.1 in training:** you do not need to. The final 1.1 miles are covered by the taper (you arrive fresh, not fatigued), the race-day adrenaline, and the crowd. Running 13 miles in training buys you nothing and costs you a week of recovery you cannot afford this close to the race.

## Workout Descriptions

### Easy Runs (the backbone - about 80% of this plan)
- **Pace:** Conversational. Full sentences. If you are breathing hard, slow down.
- **HR:** Zone 2 (60-70% max)
- **Purpose:** Build the aerobic engine. This is where fitness comes from.
- **The beginner test:** Recite a sentence out loud. If you cannot, you are running too fast.
- **It is normal for this to feel embarrassingly slow.** It is supposed to.

### Recovery Runs
- **Pace:** Slower than easy. Genuinely shuffling is fine.
- **Purpose:** Circulation and consistency, not fitness.
- **Note:** The day after a long run. If you feel wrecked, walk it or take the day off.

### Strides
- **Distance:** 100 meters, smooth acceleration to a fast-but-relaxed pace, then walk back and fully recover.
- **Purpose:** Form and leg turnover, not fitness. They are not sprints and they should never leave you out of breath.

### Steady Runs (Weeks 9-13 only)
- **Pace:** Controlled and firm - an effort you could sustain for about an hour. Roughly 10K-to-half-marathon effort.
- **HR:** Zone 3 to low Zone 4 (75-85% max)
- **Feel:** You can speak a few words at a time, not sentences.
- **Purpose:** A modest nudge to your [lactate threshold](../concepts/lactate-threshold.md), and a chance to learn what "sustainably firm" feels like.
- **If in doubt, run it too slow.** For a first half, a steady run done too easy costs you nothing. Done too hard, it costs you the next four days.

### Long Runs
- **Pace:** SLOW. 60-90 seconds per mile slower than your goal race pace.
- **Purpose:** Time on feet. Aerobic adaptation. Fueling practice. Mental rehearsal.
- **This is the most important run of the week and the one beginners most often ruin by running too fast.**
- Walking breaks in a long run are perfectly acceptable. They are not failure.

## Fueling and Hydration

**Practice race-day fueling on EVERY long run over about 90 minutes. Nothing new on race day. Ever.**

For most beginners, 90 minutes arrives somewhere around the 9-mile long run (Week 6). From then on, every long run is a fueling rehearsal.

- **Before:** Eat the same pre-run breakfast you plan to eat on race morning, 2-3 hours before. Test it. If it disagrees with you, change it now, not in Week 16.
- **During:** Take a gel, chew, or drink roughly every 30-45 minutes after the first 45 minutes. Take it with water, not with sports drink. Your gut is trainable, and this is the training.
- **After:** Carbohydrate plus protein within about 60 minutes.
- **On race day:** Use the exact products you practiced with. If the race hands out a brand you have never used, carry your own.

The single most common first-half-marathon disaster is a GI blow-up at mile 9 caused by a gel the runner had never tried before. Do not be that person.

Full detail: [Fueling and Nutrition](../fundamentals/fueling-and-nutrition.md).

## Signs You Should Back Off

Stop or reduce - do not "push through" - if you notice any of these:

**Back off immediately (take 2-3 days easy or fully off):**
- Pain that changes how you run. Any limp, any altered gait. This is non-negotiable.
- Sharp or localized pain, especially in a bone (shin, foot, hip). Diffuse muscle soreness is fine; a specific point that hurts to press is not.
- Pain that gets *worse* as the run goes on, rather than warming up and fading.
- Resting heart rate 7+ beats above your normal for several consecutive mornings.

**Reassess the week (cut volume 25-30%):**
- Easy runs feel hard at paces that felt easy two weeks ago.
- Sleep is disrupted, appetite is off, or you are irritable and flat.
- You are dreading runs you used to look forward to.
- You are getting sick repeatedly.
- Your legs feel heavy every single day, not just after long runs.

**The rule:** taking three days off costs you nothing. Running through a stress reaction costs you the race and three months. Consistency over 16 weeks beats heroism in any one week. See [injury prevention](../fundamentals/injury-prevention.md).

## If You Miss Runs, a Week, or Get Injured

**If you miss a single easy run:** Skip it. Do not make it up. Do not add the miles elsewhere. It does not matter.

**If you miss a long run:** Move it to Sunday if you can. If the week is gone, do not run the missed long run *and* the next one back to back - repeat the previous long-run distance instead of jumping ahead.

**If you miss one week (illness, travel, work):**
- Come back at roughly 70-80% of the volume you were at before the break.
- Repeat the week you missed rather than jumping ahead to where the plan says you should be.
- You have lost almost no fitness in seven days. You have lost seven days of the schedule. Those are different problems - solve the second one by moving the race or accepting a slightly smaller peak, not by cramming.

**If you miss two to three weeks:**
- Rebuild for 1-2 weeks at 60-70% volume with no steady work.
- Then rejoin the plan roughly 3-4 weeks earlier than where the calendar says you are.
- If that means the peak long run lands too close to race day, cut the peak long run to 10-11 miles and take the extra taper. A slightly under-trained runner finishes a half marathon. An injured one does not start it.

**If you miss four or more weeks, or you are injured:**
- Do not try to salvage the race by compressing the plan. This is how beginners get hurt badly.
- See a physiotherapist or sports doctor for anything that has persisted more than 10 days.
- Cross-train (bike, pool running, elliptical) to hold aerobic fitness while the injury heals - it holds far more than you would expect.
- Defer to a later race. There is always another half marathon. There is only one of your tibia.

**If you are injured mid-plan:** the plan is now irrelevant. Rehab is the plan. Come back to Week 1 when you are pain-free walking, then pain-free running 20 minutes.

## Race Week

### The Last Seven Days
- **Do not run more than the plan says.** The taper is not a suggestion. The work is done. You cannot gain fitness in seven days, and you can absolutely lose the race in seven days.
- **Do not try anything new.** No new shoes, no new gels, no new socks, no new breakfast, no new massage, no new stretching routine.
- **Sleep is the priority, especially two nights before.** The night before a race is often poor - that is fine and normal, and it is why the night before *that* one matters more.
- **Eat normally, with a modest carbohydrate emphasis for the last 2-3 days.** You do not need a dramatic carb-load for a half marathon. Just eat enough and reduce fibre a little on the final day.
- **Hydrate steadily.** Pale yellow urine. You cannot fix dehydration on race morning.

### Race Morning
- Wake 3 hours before the gun.
- Eat your tested breakfast 2-3 hours before (300-500 calories, mostly carbohydrate, low fat and fibre).
- Arrive early. The toilet queue is always longer than you think.
- Warm up: 5-10 minutes of easy jogging plus a few strides. That is all. **Beginners do not need a long warm-up - you have 13.1 miles to warm up in.**
- Line up further back than you think you should. Everyone lines up too far forward.

## Race Strategy: How to Actually Run 13.1

### The Beginner Pacing Plan (this is the whole strategy)

- **Miles 1-3: RUN SLOWER THAN GOAL PACE.** 15-30 seconds per mile slower. You will feel amazing. Everyone around you will be running away from you. Let them. Almost all of them are making a mistake and you will pass them at mile 10.
- **Miles 4-9: Settle into goal pace.** Find a rhythm. Relax the shoulders. Take your fuel on schedule, not when you feel like you need it - by the time you feel you need it, it is too late.
- **Miles 10-11: This is where the race starts.** It will get uncomfortable. This is expected and does not mean anything is wrong. Shorten your focus: get to the next mile marker.
- **Miles 12-13.1: Whatever is left.** If you have paced correctly, you will be passing people. It is a genuinely great feeling and it is the reward for the discipline of miles 1-3.

**The single biggest mistake in a first half marathon is going out too fast in the first 3 miles.** It is not close. Nothing else costs as much time. The adrenaline of the start line makes goal pace feel effortless, so you drift 30 seconds per mile faster, bank a couple of minutes, and pay it back with interest and misery from mile 10.

### Walking Is Allowed
Walking through aid stations to drink properly costs about 10-15 seconds and is often a net gain, because drinking on the run at a jostling aid station usually means wearing most of the cup. Plan for it. Walking is not failure - it is a pacing tool.

### If It Goes Wrong
- **You went out too fast and you are struggling at mile 9:** Slow down deliberately and immediately, take fuel and water, and settle into a jog. Do not stop. You will finish.
- **Cramping:** Slow down, walk briefly, take fluid and electrolyte. Do not stretch aggressively mid-race.
- **GI trouble:** Stop taking gels, stick to water, slow down. You will still finish.
- **It is hotter than expected:** Add 20-30 seconds per mile to goal pace from mile 1. Heat is a real physiological tax, not a mental one. Adjust or blow up.

For a fuller treatment: [race-day execution](../concepts/race-day-execution.md).

## After the Race

- Keep walking for 10-15 minutes. Do not sit down immediately.
- Eat and drink within 30-60 minutes.
- **Do not run for 3-5 days.** Nothing. Walk if you want to move.
- Easy running only for the following 7-10 days.
- No hard workouts for at least 2 weeks.
- Rough guide: one easy day per mile raced, so about two weeks before you think about training hard again.
- Then, if you loved it: [the 20-week beginner marathon plan](20-week-marathon-training-plan-beginner.md) is waiting.

## Customization Guidelines

### If You Can Only Run 4 Days a Week
- Drop the Sunday recovery run entirely.
- Do not redistribute those miles. Just run less. Four consistent days beats five days where the fifth one hurts you.
- Your peak week becomes roughly 29-30 miles. That is completely sufficient to finish a half marathon strong.

### If a Week Feels Too Hard
- Repeat the previous week instead of progressing. There is no penalty for spending an extra week at a given volume.
- Better to arrive at the start line a little under-trained and completely healthy than perfectly trained and injured.

### If You Are Cruising and It Feels Too Easy
- **Resist.** Do not add miles, do not add speedwork, do not run the long runs faster.
- If you genuinely have surplus capacity, add cross-training or [strength work](../fundamentals/strength-training.md), not running miles.
- Feeling good in Week 8 is what the plan is supposed to produce. It is not evidence that the plan is too easy - it is evidence that it is working.
- If you want more running, the [intermediate half plan](16-week-half-marathon-training-plan.md) exists. Consider it *next* cycle, not mid-cycle.

### If You Are Coming from a 10K
- You are in exactly the right place. Start at Week 1 anyway.
- Your speed is not the limiter for a half marathon. Your aerobic endurance is.

### Adding Strength Work
- 2x per week, 20-30 minutes: squats, lunges, single-leg work, calf raises, planks, glute bridges.
- Put it on Monday and Thursday, after the run, never before a long run.
- It is one of the highest-return injury-prevention investments a beginner can make. See [strength training](../fundamentals/strength-training.md).

## Key Takeaways

1. **Finish strong and healthy. That is the goal.** Your first half marathon is a personal best by definition. Time goals belong to the second one.
2. **Easy means easy.** If you take one thing from this plan, take this. Most beginners run their easy days too hard and pay for it for 16 weeks.
3. **Long runs are run slow - slower than goal pace.** Their job is time on your feet, not rehearsal of race speed.
4. **Volume before intensity, never both at once.** Miles first, effort later.
5. **The down week every 4th week is where you actually get fitter.** Do not skip it.
6. **Practice fueling on every long run over 90 minutes.** Nothing new on race day.
7. **Go out slow.** Miles 1-3 slower than goal pace is the entire race strategy.
8. **Consistency beats heroism.** Sixteen weeks of honest, unglamorous, mostly-easy running is what gets you across the line. There are no shortcuts and you do not need any.
