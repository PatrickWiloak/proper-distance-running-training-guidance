# 12-Week 5K Training Plan (Beginner Level)

## Plan Overview

**Target Audience:** First-time 5K runners who can currently run/walk 20-30 minutes continuously
**Goal:** Finish your first 5K, then race it
**Weekly Mileage:** 11-19 miles (peak 19)
**Running Days:** 4 per week, maximum. Three rest/cross-train days are part of the plan, not a gap in it.
**Experience Required:** None beyond being able to cover 20-30 minutes on foot, run/walk allowed

This is the entry point to the training library. If you already run 20-25 miles per week and
have raced before, skip this and use the [intermediate 5K plan](12-week-5k-training-plan.md).

## Before You Start

1. **Determine Your Current Fitness:**
   - Do NOT run a time trial in Week 1. You do not need one yet.
   - Instead, note how long you can move continuously and how it felt.
   - Around Week 8 you will have a natural fitness check (the optional tune-up), and that is
     early enough to set a race goal.

2. **Know Your Two Efforts (that is all you need for now):**
   - **Easy:** Conversational. You can speak in full sentences. This is 90% of the plan.
     See [heart-rate zones](../concepts/heart-rate-zones.md) (Zone 2, 60-70% max HR).
   - **Steady/Comfortably Hard:** Only from Week 9. You can speak 3-5 word phrases, no more.
   - You do not need a VDOT score, a lactate threshold estimate, or a GPS watch to start.

3. **Run/Walk Is Real Running:**
   - In Weeks 1-6 every run in this plan may be done as run/walk intervals.
   - Suggested starting ratio: run 4 minutes / walk 1 minute, repeated.
   - Walking breaks are a pacing tool used by experienced marathoners. They are not a failure.

4. **Schedule Your Race:**
   - Pick your goal 5K for Week 12 (Sunday).
   - Optional: a low-stakes parkrun or fun-run in Week 8 as a rehearsal.

## Training Philosophy

Everything in this plan serves one goal: **arrive at the start line healthy and consistent.**
A beginner does not get faster by training hard. A beginner gets faster by training *often*,
for months, without getting hurt. Speed is a side effect of consistency.

### The Non-Negotiables

1. **Consistency beats intensity.** Missing a whole week of training costs you more than any
   single easy run being "too slow" ever will. If you have to choose between a hard workout and
   a habit, choose the habit.
2. **Easy means EASY.** This is the single most common beginner error: running easy days too
   hard. If you cannot hold a conversation, you are running too fast. Slow down. Read
   [the easy/hard rule](../concepts/easy-hard.md) and
   [the aerobic system](../fundamentals/aerobic-system.md) to understand why the slow miles are
   the ones doing the work.
3. **Build volume BEFORE intensity.** Never raise volume and intensity in the same week. This
   plan raises mileage for eight weeks and only then adds anything that resembles speed.
4. **Every 4th week is a down week.** Weeks 4 and 8 cut volume roughly 25%. Your body adapts
   during the easy weeks, not during the hard ones. See
   [recovery](../fundamentals/recovery.md).
5. **Rest days are training.** They are on the calendar for a reason. Do not apologize for them,
   do not "make them up", do not quietly fill them with a run because you felt good.
6. **Strides before speedwork.** Short, relaxed accelerations teach your legs to move quickly
   without the injury risk of real intervals. You will do strides for six weeks before you do a
   single hard rep.

### Phase Map

- **Weeks 1-4:** Base. All easy. Run/walk encouraged. Strides introduced.
- **Weeks 5-8:** Build. More easy volume, strides twice a week, one light fartlek.
- **Weeks 9-11:** Sharpen. Gentle cruise work, then a small amount of 5K-pace running.
- **Week 12:** Taper and race.

## The Weekly Template

Every week uses the same skeleton, so it fits around a life:

- **Monday:** Rest
- **Tuesday:** Easy run (this becomes the workout day late in the plan)
- **Wednesday:** Easy run (short)
- **Thursday:** Rest or cross-train
- **Friday:** Easy run (+ strides)
- **Saturday:** Rest
- **Sunday:** Long run

Cross-training on Thursday means 30-40 minutes of easy cycling, swimming, elliptical, or a brisk
walk. It is optional. Doing nothing is also fine.

## The 12-Week Plan

### Phase 1: Base Building (Weeks 1-4)

Run/walk everything in this phase if you want to. Nothing here should leave you breathing hard.

#### Week 1: Get Started (11 miles)
- **Monday:** Rest
- **Tuesday:** Easy 3 miles (run/walk: 4 min run / 1 min walk)
- **Wednesday:** Easy 2 miles (run/walk)
- **Thursday:** Rest or 30 min cross-train
- **Friday:** Easy 2.5 miles (run/walk)
- **Saturday:** Rest
- **Sunday:** Long run 3.5 miles easy (run/walk, no time goal)

#### Week 2: Settle In (12 miles)
- **Monday:** Rest
- **Tuesday:** Easy 3 miles
- **Wednesday:** Easy 2.5 miles
- **Thursday:** Rest or 30 min cross-train
- **Friday:** Easy 2.5 miles + 4x20 second relaxed pickups (not sprints)
- **Saturday:** Rest
- **Sunday:** Long run 4 miles easy

#### Week 3: Add Strides (13 miles)
- **Monday:** Rest
- **Tuesday:** Easy 3.5 miles
- **Wednesday:** Easy 2.5 miles
- **Thursday:** Rest or 30 min cross-train
- **Friday:** Easy 3 miles + 4x100m strides
- **Saturday:** Rest
- **Sunday:** Long run 4 miles easy

#### Week 4: Recovery Week (10 miles)
Volume drops on purpose. Do not add it back.
- **Monday:** Rest
- **Tuesday:** Easy 3 miles
- **Wednesday:** Easy 2 miles
- **Thursday:** Rest
- **Friday:** Easy 2 miles + 4x100m strides
- **Saturday:** Rest
- **Sunday:** Long run 3 miles easy

---

### Phase 2: Build Volume (Weeks 5-8)

Volume rises. Intensity still does not. If you are tempted to push the pace, add a walk break
instead.

#### Week 5: More Easy Miles (14 miles)
- **Monday:** Rest
- **Tuesday:** Easy 4 miles + 6x100m strides
- **Wednesday:** Easy 3 miles
- **Thursday:** Rest or 30-40 min cross-train
- **Friday:** Easy 3 miles + 6x100m strides
- **Saturday:** Rest
- **Sunday:** Long run 4 miles easy (try running this one continuously)

#### Week 6: Extend the Long Run (15 miles)
- **Monday:** Rest
- **Tuesday:** Easy 4 miles + 6x100m strides
- **Wednesday:** Easy 3 miles
- **Thursday:** Rest or 30-40 min cross-train
- **Friday:** Easy 3 miles
- **Saturday:** Rest
- **Sunday:** Long run 5 miles easy

#### Week 7: First Taste of Faster Running (16 miles)
The fartlek below is *play*, not a workout. "Comfortably hard" means you could still say a short
sentence. If you are gasping, you have gone too fast.
- **Monday:** Rest
- **Tuesday:** **Fartlek** - 1.5mi easy warmup + 6x1 min comfortably hard w/2 min easy jog between + 1mi easy cooldown = 4.5 miles
- **Wednesday:** Easy 3 miles
- **Thursday:** Rest
- **Friday:** Easy 3.5 miles + 6x100m strides
- **Saturday:** Rest
- **Sunday:** Long run 5 miles easy

#### Week 8: Recovery + Optional Rehearsal (12 miles)
- **Monday:** Rest
- **Tuesday:** Easy 3.5 miles
- **Wednesday:** Easy 2.5 miles
- **Thursday:** Rest
- **Friday:** Easy 3 miles + 6x100m strides
- **Saturday:** Rest
- **Sunday:** **OPTIONAL: parkrun / low-key 5K at a comfortable effort** OR long run 3 miles easy

If you do the optional 5K, treat it as a dress rehearsal, not a race. Run it at an effort you
could hold for an hour. The point is to practise the routine: pinning a bib, warming up,
starting slow. Your time here is a useful reference for setting a Week 12 goal.

---

### Phase 3: Sharpen (Weeks 9-11)

Now, and only now, do we add real intensity. There is exactly one quality session per week. The
long run and every other run stay easy.

#### Week 9: Introduce Cruise Intervals (17 miles)
- **Monday:** Rest
- **Tuesday:** **Cruise Intervals** - 1.5mi warmup + 2x8 min comfortably hard w/3 min easy jog + 1.5mi cooldown = 5 miles
- **Wednesday:** Easy 3 miles
- **Thursday:** Rest or 30-40 min cross-train
- **Friday:** Easy 4 miles + 6x100m strides
- **Saturday:** Rest
- **Sunday:** Long run 5 miles easy

#### Week 10: First Real Intervals (18 miles)
Your first hard reps of the whole plan, in Week 10. That is intentional.
- **Monday:** Rest
- **Tuesday:** **Intervals** - 1.5mi warmup + 4x400m at 5K effort w/400m walk-jog recovery + cooldown = 5 miles
- **Wednesday:** Easy 3.5 miles
- **Thursday:** Rest
- **Friday:** Easy 4 miles + 6x100m strides
- **Saturday:** Rest
- **Sunday:** Long run 5.5 miles easy

#### Week 11: Race-Pace Rehearsal (19 miles)
- **Monday:** Rest
- **Tuesday:** **Goal Pace** - 1.5mi warmup + 2x1 mile at goal 5K pace w/3 min recovery jog + 1.5mi cooldown = 5.5 miles
- **Wednesday:** Easy 3.5 miles
- **Thursday:** Rest
- **Friday:** Easy 4 miles + 6x100m strides
- **Saturday:** Rest
- **Sunday:** Long run 6 miles easy

If the 2x1 mile feels impossible at your goal pace, your goal pace is wrong, not your fitness.
Adjust the goal. That is what the rehearsal is for.

---

### Phase 4: Taper + Race (Week 12)

#### Week 12: Taper Week (7 miles + race)
- **Monday:** Rest
- **Tuesday:** Easy 3 miles + 6x100m strides
- **Wednesday:** Easy 2 miles
- **Thursday:** Rest
- **Friday:** Easy 2 miles + 4x100m strides
- **Saturday:** Rest (or a 10 minute walk)
- **Sunday:** **RACE DAY! Your first 5K** 🏁

You will feel sluggish and restless this week. Everyone does. The fitness is already in the bank;
nothing you do in the last seven days can add to it, and quite a lot can subtract from it. Trust
the taper.

## Workout Descriptions

### Easy Runs
- **Pace:** Conversational. Full sentences, out loud, without gasping.
- **HR:** Zone 2 (60-70% max)
- **Purpose:** Build the aerobic engine, and recover while doing it
- **Note:** If in doubt, go slower. There is no such thing as an easy run that was too easy.

### Run/Walk Intervals
- **Ratio:** Start at run 4 min / walk 1 min. Progress toward run 9 min / walk 1 min.
- **Purpose:** Cover the planned distance at a genuinely easy effort while your tendons, bones,
  and connective tissue catch up with your lungs
- **Note:** Drop the walk breaks when they start to feel like an interruption, not a relief.

### Strides
- **Distance:** 100 meters (about 20 seconds)
- **Pace:** Smooth acceleration to roughly mile race pace, relaxed, never all-out
- **Recovery:** Walk back to the start. Full recovery.
- **Purpose:** Teach the legs to turn over quickly, improve form, prepare for real speedwork
- **Note:** Strides are not a workout. You should finish them feeling fresher, not tired.

### Fartlek (Week 7)
- **Pace:** Comfortably hard, by feel. No watch required.
- **Purpose:** A first, low-risk introduction to running faster than easy
- **Note:** Full easy-jog recovery between each. Stop the session if form falls apart.

### Cruise Intervals (Week 9)
- **Pace:** Comfortably hard, roughly the fastest pace you could hold for an hour
- **Feel:** 3-5 word phrases only
- **Purpose:** Raise your [lactate threshold](../concepts/lactate-threshold.md)
- **Note:** These should feel controlled and repeatable, never like a race.

### Intervals (Week 10)
- **Pace:** Goal 5K effort, no faster
- **Recovery:** Generous. Walk-jog until you genuinely want to run again.
- **Purpose:** Get comfortable with race-day breathing and leg speed
- **Note:** If you cannot finish the last rep at the same pace as the first, you started too fast.

### Long Runs
- **Pace:** Easy. Always easy. Never a "let's see what I've got" day.
- **Purpose:** Endurance, and the confidence that the race distance is well within reach
- **Note:** Time on feet matters more than pace or distance.

## If You Miss a Week

You will miss a week. Life happens: illness, work, travel, a sick kid. This section exists so
that missing a week does not turn into quitting.

**Missed 1-3 days:** Nothing. Pick up the plan where the calendar is today. Do not try to cram
the missed runs into the remaining days.

**Missed 1 week:** Repeat the week you missed, then continue. You have lost almost no fitness.
Your total plan is now 13 weeks, which is fine. If the race date is fixed and you cannot afford
the extra week, skip forward but cut the next week's mileage by about 20% and drop that week's
quality session to an easy run.

**Missed 2 weeks:** Go back 2 weeks in the plan and resume from there. Rebuild for 2 weeks
before resuming the progression. Do not attempt to jump back in at your pre-break volume; that is
where injuries live.

**Missed 3+ weeks:** Restart the plan from Week 1, or from Week 5 if the layoff was for reasons
other than injury and you feel fine. Adjust your race goal to "finish, comfortably." That is
still a real goal and it is still worth showing up for.

**Missed a single workout, not a week:**
1. **Missed easy run:** Skip it. It is gone. Move on.
2. **Missed long run:** Shift it to another day that week if you can. Do not stack it next to a
   quality day.
3. **Missed quality session (Weeks 9-11):** Do it 1-2 days later, or skip it. One missed session
   changes nothing.
4. **Never:** Double up two hard days to "catch up." This is how beginners get hurt.

## Signs You Should Back Off

You are allowed to change the plan. In fact you are expected to. Back off if you see any of these:

- **Pain that changes how you run.** Soreness is fine. Anything that alters your gait is not.
  Stop, and see [injury prevention](../fundamentals/injury-prevention.md).
- **Pain that gets worse during a run**, rather than warming up and fading.
- **Any sharp, localized, or one-sided pain**, especially in shin, knee, hip, or the top of the
  foot. Take days off immediately. A stress reaction caught in week one is a week off; ignored,
  it is a season.
- **Resting heart rate elevated 5+ bpm** for several mornings in a row.
- **Sleep getting worse** while training load goes up.
- **Easy pace feeling hard.** If your normal easy run suddenly requires effort, you are tired,
  not unfit.
- **Losing interest in running.** Genuinely one of the most reliable early signals of overreaching.
- **Getting sick repeatedly.**

**What "backing off" means:** take 2-3 extra rest days, or cut the week's volume in half and drop
all quality. Then resume at the previous week's volume. You will lose nothing. Pushing through
costs weeks or months.

## Success Tips

1. **Slow down.** Yes, slower than that. This is the whole game.
2. **Show up on the bad days.** A grumpy 2 miles beats a skipped 4.
3. **Shoes matter, gadgets do not.** Get fitted at a running shop. Ignore everything else.
4. **Sleep 7-9 hours.** Adaptation happens while you are asleep, not while you are running.
5. **Do not race your training partners.** Or strangers. Or your own past self on a segment.
6. **Log your runs.** A one-line note on how you felt is worth more than any metric.
7. **Two easy weeks out of twelve is not slacking.** It is the plan working.
8. **Finishing is a legitimate goal.** Racing is what you do the second time.

## Race Week Strategy

### Days Before Race:
- **5-7 days out:** Last strides. No hard running of any kind.
- **3-4 days out:** Easy running only, short.
- **2 days out:** Short easy run with a few strides.
- **1 day out:** Rest. Lay out your kit. Sleep is more valuable than any last run.

### Race Day:
- **Eat what you have already eaten before runs.** Race morning is not the time for a new
  breakfast.
- **Warm-up:** 10 min easy jog, then 4-6x100m strides. Finish the warm-up 10 minutes before the
  gun.
- **Race strategy:** The first half mile should feel almost stupidly controlled. Everybody around
  you will go out too fast. Let them. Settle into rhythm through mile 2, and if you have anything
  left in the last half mile, spend it.
- **Cool-down:** 10 min easy jog or a walk.

### After Race:
- Take 3-5 days completely easy, or off.
- No hard workouts for at least a week.
- Then decide what is next. The [beginner 10K plan](12-week-10k-training-plan-beginner.md) is the
  natural step up.

## Weekly Mileage Progression

```
Week 1:  11 miles (base, run/walk)
Week 2:  12 miles (base)
Week 3:  13 miles (base + strides)
Week 4:  10 miles (RECOVERY)
Week 5:  14 miles (build)
Week 6:  15 miles (build)
Week 7:  16 miles (build + first fartlek)
Week 8:  12 miles (RECOVERY / optional rehearsal 5K)
Week 9:  17 miles (cruise intervals)
Week 10: 18 miles (first intervals)
Week 11: 19 miles (PEAK, goal-pace rehearsal)
Week 12:  7 miles + RACE (taper)
```

Note the shape: eight weeks of pure volume before a single hard rep, and two full down weeks
inside twelve. That shape is the plan. Do not flatten it.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Consistency beats intensity, every time.** Twelve mediocre weeks beat four brilliant ones
   followed by an injury.
2. **Easy days are the training, not the filler.** Running them too hard is the defining beginner
   mistake.
3. **Volume first, intensity later.** Never both in the same week.
4. **Down weeks and rest days are where you actually get fitter.**
5. **Run/walk is running.** Use it as long as it is useful.
6. **You are allowed to adjust the plan.** Backing off early is cheap. Injury is expensive.
