# 12-Week 10K Training Plan (Intermediate Level)

## Plan Overview

**Target Audience:** Runners with 1+ year consistent running, 25-30 mpw base
**Goal:** 10K race in 12 weeks
**Weekly Mileage:** 15-50 miles (peak week 50)
**Experience Required:** Comfortable running 45+ minutes continuously, completed at least one 5K race

### If This Is More Volume Than You Want

This plan peaks at **50 miles** in its biggest week. That is a real 10K build, and
it sits at the top of the intermediate range rather than the middle of it. Every weekly
total in this plan has been checked against the workouts actually listed underneath it,
so the number above is the number you will run.

**To run this plan at roughly 40 miles per week instead:**

- Trim **4-5 miles per week off the easy days only**. Take a mile off two or three easy
  runs rather than a large chunk off one.
- **Do not cut the quality sessions** (tempo, intervals, marathon-pace work). They are the
  reason the plan works.
- **Do not cut the long run** by more than a mile or two. It is the other reason.
- If the plan still feels like too much after that, you want the beginner version, not a
  trimmed version of this one. There is no shame in that and it will make you faster than
  limping through a plan that is over your head.

The volume is a dial. The easy/hard structure is not. See
[Training Principles](../fundamentals/training-principles.md) and
[Injury Prevention](../fundamentals/injury-prevention.md).

## Before You Start

1. **Determine Your Current Fitness:**
   - Run a 5K or 10K time trial OR
   - Use recent race time (any distance)
   - Enter into McMillan or VDOT calculator

2. **Know Your Training Paces:**
   - **Easy:** Conversational pace (Zone 2, 60-70% max HR)
   - **Tempo:** Comfortably hard (~10K-HM race pace, Zone 4)
   - **Interval:** Hard effort (~5K race pace, Zone 5)
   - **Marathon Pace (MP):** Steady effort (Zone 3)

3. **Schedule Your Race:**
   - Pick your goal 10K race for Week 12
   - Optional: tune-up 5K race in Week 9

## Training Philosophy

The 10K requires a balance of stamina (aerobic base), strength (lactate threshold), and speed (VO2 max). This plan emphasizes:

- **Weeks 1-4:** Base building (volume focus)
- **Weeks 5-8:** Build phase (add tempo work)
- **Weeks 9-11:** Peak phase (tempo + intervals, race-specific)
- **Week 12:** Taper and race

**Key difference from 5K:** More emphasis on tempo runs and threshold work (10K is primarily a lactate threshold race)

## Workout Descriptions

### Easy Runs
- **Pace:** Conversational, full sentences
- **HR:** Zone 2 (60-70% max)
- **Purpose:** Build aerobic base, recover
- **Note:** Should feel genuinely easy, even "too easy"

### Strides
- **Distance:** 100 meters
- **Pace:** Smooth acceleration to mile race pace
- **Recovery:** Walk back to start
- **Purpose:** Improve form, neuromuscular coordination
- **Frequency:** 2-3x per week after easy runs

### Tempo Runs (Threshold Pace)
- **Pace:** "Comfortably hard" - roughly current 10K race pace
- **HR:** Zone 4 (80-90% max)
- **Feel:** Can speak 3-5 words at a time
- **Purpose:** Improve lactate threshold (THE KEY WORKOUT FOR 10K)
- **Note:** Should be sustainable but challenging

### Intervals (VO2 Max Pace)
- **Pace:** Current 5K race pace
- **HR:** Zone 5 (90-100% max)
- **Recovery:** Light jog, ~80% recovered before next rep
- **Purpose:** Improve VO2 max and speed endurance
- **Note:** Should feel hard but not all-out sprint

### Marathon Pace (MP) Runs
- **Pace:** Steady, controlled effort (30-45 sec/mile slower than tempo)
- **HR:** Zone 3 (70-80% max)
- **Purpose:** Build aerobic endurance at faster-than-easy pace
- **Note:** Should feel steady and sustainable

### 10K Race Pace Practice
- **Pace:** Your goal 10K race pace
- **Purpose:** Train body and mind for race effort
- **Note:** Practice fueling and pacing strategy

### Long Runs
- **Pace:** Easy, with optional marathon pace or tempo sections
- **Purpose:** Build endurance and mental toughness
- **Note:** Duration matters more than pace

### Recovery Runs
- **Pace:** Very easy, slower than regular easy pace
- **Purpose:** Active recovery, promote blood flow
- **Note:** Day after hard workouts

## 10K Racing Strategy

### Race Week Preparation:
- **7 days out:** Last hard workout (Wednesday sharpener)
- **4-5 days out:** Easy running only
- **2-3 days out:** Short easy run with strides
- **1 day out:** Rest or very short shakeout

### Race Day Warm-Up:
- 15-20 min easy jogging
- 6-8x100m strides (building to race pace)
- Dynamic stretches/drills
- 5 min before start: final strides

### Race Pacing Strategy:
- **Mile 1:** Controlled start, 2-3 sec/mile slower than goal pace (avoid going out too fast!)
- **Miles 2-4:** Settle into goal race pace, find rhythm
- **Miles 5-6:** Push the pace, increase effort
- **Last 0.2 miles:** Empty the tank, all-out finish

### Post-Race:
- Cool-down: 10-15 min easy jog
- Recovery: Easy running for 3-5 days
- No hard workouts for at least 1 week

## Customization Guidelines

### If You Need to Miss a Workout:
1. **Miss easy run:** Skip it, no problem
2. **Miss long run:** Try to reschedule, important for endurance
3. **Miss tempo run:** Most important workout - reschedule if possible
4. **Miss intervals:** Can skip one, don't double up next week
5. **Never:** Stack quality workouts on consecutive days

### If You're Too Tired:
- Convert any hard workout to easy run
- Take extra rest day
- Recovery > one workout
- Check if you're running easy days truly easy

### If Workouts Feel Too Easy:
- Check that you calculated paces correctly
- Don't increase volume AND intensity together
- Can add 2-3 miles to easy runs
- Can extend long run by 1-2 miles
- Be cautious increasing tempo/interval volume

### If Workouts Feel Too Hard:
- **Most common issue:** Easy runs too fast (slow down!)
- Reduce tempo/interval pace 5-10 sec/mile
- Add extra recovery day between quality sessions
- Verify paces match current fitness (not goal fitness)
- Consider you may be over-trained

## Success Tips

1. **Tempo runs are king for 10K** - Don't skip these
2. **Easy days truly easy** - This allows quality days to be quality
3. **10K pace practice** - Get comfortable with race pace
4. **Nutrition matters** - Practice race-day fueling (most runners don't need fuel during 10K, but test pre-race meal)
5. **Sleep 7-9 hours** - Recovery is when adaptation happens
6. **Trust the taper** - Week 12 volume reduction is intentional
7. **Pacing discipline** - Don't go out too fast in the race
8. **Stay consistent** - Better to complete 90% of plan healthy than 100% injured

## Training Intensity Distribution

This plan follows the 80/20 principle:
- **~75% easy running** (Zones 1-2)
- **~15% threshold/tempo** (Zone 4) - higher than 5K plan
- **~10% intervals** (Zone 5)

The 10K is primarily a **lactate threshold race**, so tempo work is emphasized more than pure VO2 max intervals.

## Key Differences from 5K Training

- **More weekly mileage:** 30-45 vs 25-35
- **More tempo emphasis:** 10K = threshold race
- **Longer long runs:** 12-14 miles vs 8-10 miles
- **Less interval work:** Still important but secondary to tempo
- **Marathon pace segments:** Build aerobic endurance

## Expected Adaptations

By the end of this plan, you should:
- Raise your lactate threshold significantly
- Increase aerobic capacity and endurance
- Improve running economy
- Develop mental toughness for 10K distance
- Be able to sustain 10K pace comfortably
