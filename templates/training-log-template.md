# Weekly Training Log Template

Copy this page into a new file for each training week (or print it), fill it in, and keep the old ones.

## Why bother logging

**You cannot diagnose a training-load error in hindsight if you never wrote the load down.**

Almost every running injury and every case of burnout has the same forensic signature: a spike in load, or a niggle that was ignored for a fortnight, or a run of bad sleep alongside a heavy week. All of these are obvious in a log and invisible in memory. Memory reliably tells you the week you got hurt was "normal", because memory is not a training record. Your log is.

A log also does three quieter jobs:

- It tells you what your **actual** intensity distribution is, not what you intended it to be. Most runners who believe they run 80/20 are running considerably more moderate work than that - and the log is the only thing that will say so. See [The Easy/Hard Rule](../concepts/easy-hard.md).
- It records the **early warnings** - resting heart rate creeping up, a niggle logged three times in ten days, sleep quietly falling apart - which are only signals if you can see them in sequence. See [Injury Prevention](../fundamentals/injury-prevention.md).
- It shows you **progress you cannot feel**. The easy pace you were running in January, at the same heart rate, is usually a much more honest measure of improvement than how fast you feel.

Two rules make a log work: fill it in on the day (not on Sunday from memory), and be honest about the runs that went badly. A log that only records good weeks is a scrapbook.

**Filling it in:**

- **Planned** - what the plan said (e.g. "Easy 5mi", "6 x 800m @ I pace", "Rest").
- **Actual** - what you did. If they differ, that difference is the most valuable data in the log.
- **Pace** - average pace, or the rep pace for a workout (e.g. "3:42/800").
- **RPE** - honest effort 1-10. Easy = 3-4, marathon pace = 5-6, threshold = 7, intervals = 8-9, all-out = 10. If your "easy" runs are logging 5-6, that is the finding of the week.
- **Notes** - weather, terrain, how the legs felt, what you ate, who you ran with, what hurt. Short is fine. The notes column is where you will later find the reason a week went wrong.

**Example row:**

| Date | Planned | Actual | Distance | Pace | Avg HR | RPE | Notes |
|------|---------|--------|----------|------|--------|-----|-------|
| Tue 14 | 4 x 1mi @ T | 4 x 1mi @ T, 1min jog | 8.2 | 6:52 avg reps | 168 | 7 | Windy, last rep 5s slow. Legs good. |

## Week in Review

Answer these at the end of every week. Five minutes, honestly.

**1. What went well?**

> _(One session, one decision, one thing that worked. Name it so you repeat it.)_

**2. What did I skip or change, and why?**

> _(Life, weather, fatigue, injury, laziness? Be specific. "Skipped Thursday - third bad night's sleep" is data. "Skipped Thursday" is not.)_

**3. Was my easy running actually easy?**

> _(Check the RPE and HR columns, not your memory. If more than about a fifth of your mileage was moderate, fix that before you change anything else in the plan.)_

**4. Any niggle to watch?**

> _(Anything from the niggle table that is the same or worse than last week. What are you going to do about it this week - not next week?)_

**5. What am I adjusting next week?**

> _(Volume up/down? Move a workout? Extra rest day? Write the decision down so future-you knows why the plan changed.)_

**6. One line for the record:**

> _(How the week felt, in a sentence. These are the lines you will read back in six months.)_
