# About the Author

This guide is written by **Patrick Wiloak**.

I ran the 2013 Boston Marathon in **2:55:13** at age 20. I also ran a 4:40 mile, a
16:40 5K, and a 1:23 half marathon, and I have finished five marathons.

I am telling you that up front for one reason: so that when this guide says
*run your easy runs slower*, you know it is coming from someone who was fast
enough to ignore that advice, ignored it, and paid for it.

**First half 1:27:26. Second half 1:27:47.** A positive split of 21 seconds over
26.2 miles.

Every 5K segment of that race falls between 20:23 and 21:02 - a 39-second spread
across the whole marathon. The two slowest segments are 30-35K and 35-40K, which
is exactly where the race normally comes apart, and they are 21:02 and 20:52. I did
not hit the wall. Boston is a net-downhill course that chews up quadriceps and
produces positive splits as a matter of routine, which makes an even one worth
something.

That race is the worked example behind
[Race Day Execution](./concepts/race-day-execution.md). It is what the advice in
this guide looks like when it is actually carried out.

### A note on that day

My brother and I both finished before the bombs went off. I did not know anything had
happened until I saw it on a television in the airport. Our flight took off, and they
grounded everything right after us.

It was a great race for me. It was a tragedy for others. I try to keep both of those
things in mind, and to remember how lucky I am.

The best way I know to pay that back is to give the gift of running to other people.
That is a large part of why this guide exists.

**#BostonStrong**

## The mistake in the middle of those numbers

Run that PR ladder through the VDOT model (see
[VDOT and Pace Tables](./reference/vdot-and-pace-tables.md), where the arithmetic
is shown and verified) and a pattern falls out that is not flattering:

| Distance | Time | Implied VDOT |
|---|---|---|
| 400m | 0:54 | **78** |
| ½ mile | 2:03 | 70 |
| 1 mile | 4:40 | 64 |
| 2 mile | 10:00 | 64 |
| 5K | 16:40 | 62 |
| 10K | 36:45 | 57 |
| Half marathon | 1:23:00 | 56 |
| Marathon | 2:55:13 | **55** |

VDOT is a single number for current racing fitness. If a runner is evenly
developed, the number stays roughly flat across distances. **Mine falls off a
cliff: 78 at 400m down to 55 at the marathon.**

That is the signature of a runner with a big engine for speed and an
under-built aerobic base. And it has a concrete price. My 16:40 5K solves to a VDOT
of **61.6**, and a VDOT of 61.6 predicts a marathon of **2:39:46**.

I ran 2:55:13.

**Fifteen minutes.** That is the gap between the marathon I had the raw speed to
run and the marathon I actually ran, and the ladder shows precisely where it
leaks away: the steepest drop is between 5K and 10K, right at the point where
raw speed stops mattering and aerobic endurance takes over.

I was a miler who showed up to a marathon. I trained hard, I trained fast, and I
did not train *easy* enough or *long* enough for the distance I had entered. The
sections of this guide that will feel most tedious to a competitive runner - the
[Aerobic System](./fundamentals/aerobic-system.md), the
[Easy/Hard Rule](./concepts/easy-hard.md),
[Progression and Periodization](./fundamentals/progression.md) - are the ones I
skimped on, and my splits are the receipt.

**This guide is what I wish someone had handed me at nineteen.** Not because I ran
a fast time, but because I can show you, in my own race data, the fifteen minutes
that the fundamentals were worth.

## What that means for how you read this

- The **fundamentals are not the boring part before the real training.** They *are*
  the real training. I have the splits to prove that skipping them costs you more
  than any workout gains you back.
- **Being fast at one distance does not make you fast at the next one up.** The
  VDOT ladder above is what that looks like when it is measured.
- **I am not a coach, a physiologist, or a doctor.** I am a runner who read the
  research and made the mistakes. Everything here is cited back to the actual
  literature so that you can check me rather than trust me. Where the evidence is
  weak or contested, this guide says so.
