# McMillan Running

A comprehensive, race-focused training system developed by coach Greg McMillan. Known for the popular McMillan Running Calculator and practical, accessible training approaches.

## Who is Greg McMillan?

**Greg McMillan** is an elite running coach and exercise scientist:
- MS in Exercise Science
- Coached runners from beginners to Olympic Trials qualifiers
- Founded McMillan Running in 1999
- Created the widely-used McMillan Running Calculator
- Author of *You (Only Faster)*

**Philosophy:** Train smarter, not just harder. Individualized, race-specific training.

## The McMillan Calculator

### What It Does:

**Input one recent race time → Get training paces for all workout types**

Similar to Jack Daniels' VDOT, but with different terminology and some unique features:

1. Enter recent race time (any distance)
2. Calculator determines current fitness level
3. Provides training paces:
   - Easy run pace
   - Long run pace
   - Tempo run pace
   - VO2 max pace
   - Speed form pace
4. Also predicts equivalent race times at other distances

**Example:**
- Input: 10K in 42:00
- Calculator provides:
  - Recovery/Easy: 8:04-8:43/mile
  - Long run: 7:44-8:14/mile
  - Tempo: 6:38-6:52/mile
  - VO2 max: 6:04-6:18/mile
  - Speed form: 5:38-5:52/mile
- Predicts: 5K in 20:15, Half in 1:32:30, Marathon in 3:05

## McMillan Training Paces

### 1. Recovery Run Pace

**Purpose:** Active recovery, easy aerobic running

**Feel:** Very comfortable, can hold full conversation

**When to use:**
- Day after hard workout
- Easy days between quality sessions
- Short runs during high-mileage weeks

**Characteristics:**
- Slowest of all paces
- True recovery effort
- 60-70% of max heart rate

**Duration:** 20-45 minutes typically

**Key point:** Even easier than standard easy pace - meant for genuine recovery

---

### 2. Easy/Base Run Pace

**Purpose:** Build aerobic capacity, accumulate mileage

**Feel:** Comfortable, conversational

**When to use:**
- Most common training pace
- 70-80% of weekly mileage
- General aerobic development

**Characteristics:**
- Sustainable for hours
- Comfortable effort
- 65-75% of max heart rate

**Duration:** 30 minutes to 2+ hours

**Equivalent to:** Jack Daniels E pace

---

### 3. Long Run Pace

**Purpose:** Build endurance for goal race

**Feel:** Comfortable to moderate

**When to use:**
- Weekly long run
- Endurance-building sessions

**Characteristics:**
- Slightly faster than easy pace for many runners
- Can vary based on goal (easier for recovery long runs, faster for progressive long runs)
- 70-78% max heart rate

**Duration:** 90 minutes to 3+ hours (depending on goal race)

**McMillan variation:** Often suggests long runs faster than traditional "easy" pace, especially for marathoners

---

### 4. Tempo Run Pace

**Purpose:** Improve lactate threshold

**Feel:** "Comfortably hard"

**When to use:**
- Threshold workouts
- Most important workout for 10K-marathon training
- Once per week in quality phases

**Types:**
- **Steady tempo:** 20-40 minutes continuous
- **Cruise intervals:** Repeated tempo segments with short recovery
- **Tempo runs:** Longer sustained efforts (up to 60 minutes for advanced)

**Characteristics:**
- Can speak in short phrases
- Sustainable for 50-70 minutes
- 80-88% max heart rate

**Volume:** 20-60 minutes total at tempo pace per week

**Equivalent to:** Jack Daniels T pace, roughly 10K-half marathon race pace

---

### 5. VO2 Max Pace (Interval Pace)

**Purpose:** Improve maximal oxygen uptake

**Feel:** Hard effort

**When to use:**
- Interval workouts
- Building speed for 5K-10K
- Once every 7-14 days in quality phases

**Typical workouts:**
- 5-6 x 1000m with 2-3 min recovery
- 4-5 x 1200m with 2.5-3 min recovery
- 3-4 x 1600m with 3-4 min recovery

**Characteristics:**
- Roughly current 5K race pace
- Intervals last 3-6 minutes
- Equal or longer recovery than work interval
- 92-98% max heart rate

**Volume:** 8-12 minutes total for beginners, up to 30 minutes for advanced

**Equivalent to:** Jack Daniels I pace

---

### 6. Speed Form Pace

**Purpose:** Develop speed, running economy, neuromuscular power

**Feel:** Fast but controlled

**When to use:**
- Speed development
- Running economy work
- Final race sharpening
- Strides after easy runs

**Typical workouts:**
- 10-12 x 400m with equal recovery
- 8-10 x 200m with full recovery
- Strides: 6-8 x 100m after easy runs
- Hill sprints

**Characteristics:**
- Mile to 3K race pace
- Short repeats (200m-800m)
- Full or near-full recovery
- Fast but not all-out

**Volume:** 1-3 miles total per session

**Equivalent to:** Jack Daniels R pace

## The Three Training Systems

McMillan organizes training into three interconnected systems:

### 1. Stamina System

**Focus:** Aerobic endurance and capacity

**Primary workouts:**
- Easy runs
- Long runs
- Recovery runs

**Goal:** Build the aerobic engine - mitochondria, capillaries, fat oxidation

**Percentage of training:** 70-80%

**Key principle:** This is your foundation. Can't build speed without stamina.

---

### 2. Strength System

**Focus:** Lactate threshold and muscular endurance

**Primary workouts:**
- Tempo runs
- Cruise intervals
- Long runs with tempo finish
- Lactate clearance runs

**Goal:** Improve your body's ability to clear lactate and sustain harder efforts

**Percentage of training:** 10-15%

**Key principle:** Most important system for 10K to marathon racing

---

### 3. Speed System

**Focus:** VO2 max, speed, and neuromuscular power

**Primary workouts:**
- VO2 max intervals
- Speed form work (repetitions)
- Strides
- Hill sprints

**Goal:** Develop top-end speed and running economy

**Percentage of training:** 5-10%

**Key principle:** Important for all distances, but more critical for 5K-10K

---

## Training Phases

### Phase 1: Base Training (8-12 weeks)

**Focus:** Stamina system development

**Workouts:**
- Mostly easy and long runs
- Build weekly mileage gradually
- Some tempo work for experienced runners
- Strides 2-3x per week

**Goal:** Build aerobic foundation and prepare body for harder training

---

### Phase 2: Build Training (6-8 weeks)

**Focus:** Introduce strength and speed systems

**Workouts:**
- Maintain stamina work (70% of training)
- Add weekly tempo run (strength)
- Add VO2 max work every 10-14 days (speed)
- Continue long runs

**Goal:** Develop lactate threshold and VO2 max while maintaining base

---

### Phase 3: Race-Specific Training (6-10 weeks)

**Focus:** Prepare for goal race distance

**For 5K:**
- Emphasis on speed system (VO2 max, speed form)
- Maintain some tempo work
- 5K race pace practice

**For 10K:**
- Balance of strength (tempo) and speed (VO2 max)
- 10K race pace work

**For Half Marathon:**
- Emphasis on strength system (tempo)
- Some speed work for sharpness
- Half marathon pace practice in long runs

**For Marathon:**
- Emphasis on stamina (long runs) and strength (tempo)
- Marathon pace work in long runs
- Limited speed work

**Goal:** Build race-specific fitness

---

### Phase 4: Peak/Taper (1-3 weeks)

**Focus:** Rest and sharpen

**Workouts:**
- Reduce volume 30-50%
- Maintain some intensity at shorter duration
- Race pace touches
- Lots of rest

**Goal:** Arrive fresh and ready to race

---

## Unique McMillan Concepts

### 1. The Workout Hierarchy

**McMillan emphasizes doing workouts in order of importance:**

**For Marathon:**
1. Long run (non-negotiable)
2. Tempo run (critical)
3. Easy runs (foundation)
4. VO2 max work (optional, for sharpness)

**For 10K:**
1. Tempo run (most important)
2. VO2 max intervals (critical)
3. Easy runs (foundation)
4. Long run (important but less than marathon)

**Practical application:** If you have to skip a workout, skip the least important one.

### 2. Lactate Clearance Runs

**Unique McMillan workout:**
- Start with 20-30 min easy
- Surge for 60-90 seconds at tempo-to-VO2 max effort
- Recover 60-90 seconds easy
- Repeat 8-12 times
- Finish with easy running

**Purpose:** Teach body to clear lactate quickly during race surges

**When to use:** Race-specific phase for 10K and below

### 3. The "Just Finish" Long Run vs. "Simulator" Long Run

**Just Finish:**
- Easy pace throughout
- Focus on time on feet
- Good for building base or recovery weeks

**Simulator:**
- Practice race conditions
- Include tempo or marathon pace segments
- Mimic race day fueling
- Mentally prepare for race effort

**McMillan approach:** Use both types strategically based on training phase

### 4. Reverse Taper

**For time-crunched runners:**
- Instead of long traditional base phase
- Start with moderate mileage + quality work
- Gradually build volume over time
- Works for runners with solid fitness base

**Note:** Not recommended for beginners or those coming off injury

## Sample Training Weeks

### Beginner - 5K Training (25 miles/week, Race-Specific Phase)

- **Monday:** Rest or easy 3 miles
- **Tuesday:** VO2 max - 2 mile warmup + 6 x 800m at VO2 max pace with 2-3 min recovery + 1 mile cooldown = 6 miles
- **Wednesday:** Easy 4 miles
- **Thursday:** Tempo - 1 mile warmup + 15 min at tempo pace + 1 mile cooldown = 4 miles
- **Friday:** Easy 3 miles + 6x100m strides
- **Saturday:** Long run 8 miles at easy pace
- **Sunday:** Rest

**Total:** 25 miles (72% stamina, 20% strength/speed, 8% warmup/cooldown)

---

### Intermediate - Half Marathon Training (45 miles/week, Race-Specific Phase)

- **Monday:** Easy 6 miles
- **Tuesday:** Tempo - 2 mile warmup + 2 x 4 miles at tempo pace with 2 min recovery + 2 mile cooldown = 12 miles
- **Wednesday:** Easy 6 miles
- **Thursday:** Easy 7 miles
- **Friday:** Easy 5 miles + 8x100m strides
- **Saturday:** Long run 12 miles (first 8 easy, last 4 at half marathon pace)
- **Sunday:** Recovery 4 miles

**Total:** 45 miles (70% stamina, 25% strength, 5% speed)

---

### Advanced - Marathon Training (65 miles/week, Peak Phase)

- **Monday:** Easy 8 miles
- **Tuesday:** Tempo - 3 mile warmup + 8 miles at tempo pace + 2 mile cooldown = 13 miles
- **Wednesday:** Easy 8 miles
- **Thursday:** Easy 9 miles (AM: 6 miles, PM: 3 miles)
- **Friday:** Easy 6 miles + 6x100m strides
- **Saturday:** Long run 20 miles (first 14 easy, last 6 at marathon pace)
- **Sunday:** Recovery 8 miles

**Total:** 65 miles (75% stamina, 20% strength, 5% strides/warmup)

## Key Principles

### 1. Train Your Weaknesses

- Identify limiting system (stamina, strength, or speed)
- Emphasize that system in training
- Don't neglect others completely

### 2. Race-Specific Training

- Training should match race demands
- Marathon training ≠ 5K training
- Adjust system emphasis based on goal race

### 3. Progressive Overload

- Build gradually in each phase
- Don't rush the process
- Consistent, patient progression beats aggressive plans

### 4. Recovery is Training

- Easy days truly easy
- Recovery runs serve a purpose
- Adaptation happens during rest

### 5. Individualization

- Calculator provides starting point
- Adjust based on your response
- What works for one runner may not work for another

## Comparing McMillan to Jack Daniels

### Similarities:
- Both use race times to determine training paces
- Both emphasize easy running as foundation
- Both define specific workout paces
- Both are scientifically grounded

### Differences:

**McMillan:**
- More race-specific approach
- Three systems (stamina, strength, speed)
- More emphasis on long run variations
- Unique workouts (lactate clearance runs)
- More accessible language for recreational runners

**Jack Daniels:**
- Five specific paces (E, M, T, I, R)
- VDOT tables more comprehensive
- More prescriptive about workout volumes
- Stronger academic/research foundation
- More specific phase-by-phase guidance

**Bottom line:** Both excellent systems. Choose based on which resonates with you.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Use the calculator** - Enter recent race time to get personalized training paces

2. **Three systems approach** - Stamina (aerobic base), Strength (threshold), Speed (VO2 max and economy)

3. **Race-specific training** - Emphasize the systems most important for your goal race

4. **Easy running foundation** - 70-80% of training at recovery and easy pace

5. **Workout hierarchy** - Know which workouts matter most for your goal race

6. **Progressive phases** - Base → Build → Race-Specific → Taper

7. **Individualize** - Calculator provides starting point; adjust based on response

8. **Recovery counts** - Recovery runs have a purpose; don't skip them

**Remember:** McMillan's system is practical and accessible while being scientifically sound. Use the calculator, follow the phases, train the three systems, and you'll be ready to race.
