# Comparing Training Systems

Now that you understand the fundamentals and two major training systems (Jack Daniels and McMillan), let's compare them and help you choose the right approach.

## Quick Comparison Table

| Feature | Jack Daniels | McMillan |
|---------|-------------|----------|
| **Core Metric** | VDOT | Race time input |
| **Training Paces** | 5 paces (E, M, T, I, R) | 6 paces (Recovery, Easy, Long Run, Tempo, VO2 max, Speed Form) |
| **Framework** | Pace-based phases | Three systems (Stamina, Strength, Speed) |
| **Best For** | Structured, data-driven runners | Race-focused, practical runners |
| **Complexity** | Moderate to high | Low to moderate |
| **Resources** | Book with detailed tables | Online calculator + app |
| **Scientific Foundation** | Strong academic research | Exercise science + coaching experience |
| **Flexibility** | Provides framework, user customizes | More prescriptive race plans |
| **Beginner-Friendly** | Moderate (requires book study) | High (intuitive calculator) |

## The Common Ground

### What Both Systems Agree On:

1. **80/20 training distribution**
   - 70-80% easy running
   - 20-30% quality work
   - Both emphasize aerobic base

2. **Specific training paces**
   - Use recent race times to determine paces
   - Don't guess at workout intensities
   - Each pace serves a distinct purpose

3. **Progressive phases**
   - Base building before intensity
   - Build phase adds quality
   - Race-specific phase sharpens
   - Taper before goal race

4. **Quality over quantity**
   - Hard workouts should be truly hard
   - Easy workouts should be truly easy
   - Avoid "moderate" intensity

5. **Recovery is essential**
   - Never do hard workouts consecutively
   - Recovery runs have purpose
   - Adaptation happens during rest

6. **Individualization matters**
   - Calculators provide starting points
   - Adjust based on response
   - One size doesn't fit all

**Bottom line:** The fundamentals are the same. The differences are in organization and presentation.

## Jack Daniels' Running Formula

### Strengths:

**1. Comprehensive VDOT System**
- Single metric tracks all fitness improvements
- Updates with each new race performance
- Consistent across all training paces

**2. Scientific Rigor**
- Decades of exercise physiology research
- Precise workout prescriptions
- Well-validated approach

**3. Specific Volume Limits**
- Clear guidelines for how much quality work
- Prevents overtraining from excess intensity
- Example: Max 10K worth of I pace per session

**4. Detailed Workout Examples**
- Book provides extensive workout library
- Specific recovery intervals
- Clear progressions

**5. Works for All Distances**
- 800m to marathon
- Same framework scales across goals

### Limitations:

**1. Requires Study**
- Need to read the book thoroughly
- VDOT tables can be overwhelming
- Learning curve for beginners

**2. Less Day-to-Day Structure**
- Provides pace guidelines, not complete plans
- Users must design weekly schedules
- More work to implement

**3. Can Feel Abstract**
- Five paces might seem complicated initially
- "VDOT 52" less intuitive than "10K in 42 minutes"

**4. Limited Online Resources**
- Must buy book for full tables
- Fewer free resources compared to McMillan

### Best For:

- Runners who like data and details
- Those who want to understand the "why" behind workouts
- Experienced runners comfortable customizing plans
- Runners training for multiple distances
- People who enjoy reading running science

### How to Use:

1. Buy *Daniels' Running Formula*
2. Run a race or time trial
3. Look up VDOT in tables
4. Find your training paces for E, M, T, I, R
5. Follow phase structure from book
6. Design weekly training around your schedule and life
7. Update VDOT after key races

## McMillan Running

### Strengths:

**1. User-Friendly Calculator**
- Free online tool
- Instant training pace results
- Very accessible for beginners

**2. Race-Specific Approach**
- Clear focus on goal race
- Workout hierarchy for each distance
- Easy to prioritize key workouts

**3. Three Systems Framework**
- Stamina, Strength, Speed easier to conceptualize
- Intuitive organization
- Clear emphasis for each race distance

**4. Practical Coaching Wisdom**
- Blog articles answer real-world questions
- Troubleshooting common issues
- More "coach speak" than "scientist speak"

**5. Complete Training Plans Available**
- Can purchase detailed plans for specific races
- Day-by-day structure
- Less customization work required

**6. Unique Workouts**
- Lactate clearance runs
- Simulator long runs
- More variety in workout types

### Limitations:

**1. Less Detailed on Volume Limits**
- Doesn't prescribe exact quality limits as precisely as Daniels
- More left to user judgment or coach guidance

**2. Requires Paid Resources for Full Value**
- Calculator is free but basic
- Detailed plans and coaching require subscription
- Book is helpful but not as comprehensive as Daniels

**3. Less Academic Detail**
- More practical, less theoretical
- Some runners want deeper scientific explanations

### Best For:

- Beginners wanting simple, clear guidance
- Runners focused on one specific goal race
- Those who prefer coaching approach over academic approach
- Runners who want complete day-by-day plans
- People overwhelmed by too much detail

### How to Use:

1. Go to mcmillanrunning.com
2. Enter recent race time in calculator
3. Get training paces
4. Follow training phase structure (Base → Build → Race-Specific → Taper)
5. Emphasize appropriate system for your goal race
6. Optional: Purchase detailed plan or app subscription
7. Update paces after races

## Other Notable Training Approaches

### Hanson's Marathon Method

**Philosophy:** "Train slower to race faster"

**Key Features:**
- 16-week marathon-specific plan
- Caps long run at 16 miles (controversial)
- High weekly mileage (55-70 mpw)
- Emphasis on cumulative fatigue
- Quality work on tired legs

**Best for:** Experienced marathoners comfortable with high mileage and running quality workouts on less-than-fresh legs

**Different from Daniels/McMillan:** Lower long run volume, higher overall weekly volume, more cumulative fatigue approach

---

### Pfitzinger (Advanced Marathoning)

**Philosophy:** High mileage with specific marathon preparation

**Key Features:**
- Multiple plan levels (55-85+ mpw)
- Medium-long runs (MLRs) in addition to long runs
- Lactate threshold emphasis
- VO2 max workouts
- Marathon pace work in long runs

**Best for:** Advanced marathoners ready for high volume and sophisticated workouts

**Different from Daniels/McMillan:** More prescriptive weekly structure, higher volume, more emphasis on medium-long runs

---

### Lydiard System

**Philosophy:** Build huge aerobic base, then sharpen

**Key Features:**
- Very long base phase (12-16+ weeks)
- Extremely high easy mileage
- Short, intense sharpening phase
- Hill training phase
- Proven with elite runners

**Best for:** Dedicated runners with time for long base building, those willing to postpone intensity work

**Different from Daniels/McMillan:** Much longer base phase, less ongoing quality work during base, more cyclical periodization

---

### 80/20 Running (Matt Fitzgerald)

**Philosophy:** Strict 80% low intensity, 20% high intensity

**Key Features:**
- Very explicit about intensity distribution
- Zone-based training (heart rate or pace)
- Emphasizes polarized training research
- Simpler than Daniels, similar to McMillan

**Best for:** Runners who want simple intensity guidelines, those who like heart rate training

**Different from Daniels/McMillan:** More rigid about 80/20 split, less focus on specific pace prescriptions, more emphasis on intensity distribution

---

## Which System Should You Choose?

### For Beginners (0-1 year of running):

**Recommendation: McMillan**

**Why:**
- Calculator is immediately accessible
- Simple framework (three systems)
- Free resources to get started
- Less overwhelming than detailed tables

**How to start:**
1. Run a 5K race or time trial
2. Use McMillan calculator
3. Focus on stamina system (easy runs, long runs)
4. Add strength work (tempo) after 3-6 months
5. Keep it simple

---

### For Intermediate Runners (1-3 years):

**Recommendation: Either works, slight edge to Jack Daniels**

**Why:**
- Ready to understand deeper principles
- Benefit from precise volume guidelines
- Can handle customization
- VDOT system tracks improvement well

**But McMillan still works great if you prefer:**
- Race-specific plans
- Simpler framework
- Less customization work

**How to choose:**
- Like data and details? → Jack Daniels
- Want ready-made plans? → McMillan
- Can't decide? Try both, see which resonates

---

### For Advanced Runners (3+ years):

**Recommendation: Jack Daniels, or combine approaches**

**Why:**
- Appreciate scientific precision
- Benefit from specific volume limits
- Can customize effectively
- Deeper understanding helps fine-tuning

**Consider combining:**
- Use Daniels VDOT for pace determination
- Apply McMillan's race-specific workout hierarchy
- Add elements from Pfitzinger or Hansons if appropriate

---

### For Marathon Runners Specifically:

**Recommendation: McMillan or Pfitzinger**

**Why:**
- McMillan emphasizes stamina + strength systems perfectly for marathon
- Pfitzinger plans are marathon-specific with proven structure
- Daniels works too but less marathon-specific detail

**Consider:**
- Beginner marathoner: McMillan
- Intermediate: McMillan or Pfitzinger 55-70 mpw
- Advanced: Pfitzinger 70-85+ mpw or custom plan

---

### For 5K/10K Runners Specifically:

**Recommendation: Jack Daniels**

**Why:**
- Very precise I pace and R pace prescriptions
- Better guidance on VO2 max work
- Volume limits prevent overdoing speed work

**McMillan works too:**
- Good workout hierarchy for these distances
- Calculator provides all needed paces

---

## Practical Decision Framework

### Ask Yourself:

**1. Do I like details and data?**
- Yes → Jack Daniels
- No → McMillan

**2. Do I want complete plans or frameworks?**
- Complete plans → McMillan (purchase plans)
- Frameworks to customize → Jack Daniels

**3. What's my goal race?**
- 5K-10K → Slight edge to Jack Daniels
- Half marathon → Either works great
- Marathon → Slight edge to McMillan or Pfitzinger

**4. How experienced am I?**
- Beginner → McMillan
- Intermediate → Either
- Advanced → Jack Daniels or combined approach

**5. What's my budget?**
- Free or book only → Jack Daniels (buy book once)
- Willing to pay for plans/app → McMillan offers more online

**6. How much time do I have for planning?**
- Lots of time → Jack Daniels (study and customize)
- Limited time → McMillan (use ready-made plans)

## Can You Use Both?

**Absolutely!**

### Hybrid Approach:

1. **Use VDOT for pace determination**
   - More comprehensive tables
   - Precise workout paces

2. **Apply McMillan's three systems thinking**
   - Easy to conceptualize training emphasis
   - Good for race-specific planning

3. **Follow Daniels' volume guidelines**
   - Clear limits on quality work
   - Prevents overtraining

4. **Use McMillan's workout variety**
   - Lactate clearance runs
   - Simulator long runs

**Many successful runners cherry-pick from multiple systems.**

## The Most Important Thing

### All These Systems Work IF:

1. **You follow them consistently**
   - Showing up matters more than perfect plan
   - 6 months of McMillan beats 6 weeks of Daniels

2. **You respect easy days**
   - Every system emphasizes 70-80% easy
   - This is non-negotiable

3. **You're patient**
   - Results come in months and years
   - Trust the process

4. **You listen to your body**
   - Calculators give guidelines, not commandments
   - Adjust for reality

5. **You stay healthy**
   - Consistent moderate training beats aggressive injury-prone training
   - Longevity matters

**The best system is the one you'll actually follow.**

## Key Takeaways

1. **Both Daniels and McMillan are excellent, evidence-based systems** - You can't go wrong with either

2. **They agree on fundamentals** - 80/20 training, specific paces, progressive phases, recovery

3. **Daniels is more scientific and detailed** - Better for data-driven runners who like customization

4. **McMillan is more practical and accessible** - Better for beginners or those wanting ready-made plans

5. **Choose based on your personality and goals** - Both work; pick what resonates with you

6. **You can combine approaches** - Take the best elements from each

7. **Consistency beats perfection** - Following any good plan is better than plan-hopping

8. **The fundamentals matter most** - Easy runs easy, hard runs hard, progressive overload, recovery

**Remember:** Understanding the principles from the Fundamentals section is more important than which specific system you follow. If you grasp why you're doing each workout, you can succeed with any well-designed training approach.
