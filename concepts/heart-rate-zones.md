# Heart Rate Zones

Training by heart rate helps you run at the right intensity for each workout. Understanding heart rate zones ensures you're easy runs are actually easy and your hard workouts are hard enough.

## Why Use Heart Rate?

**Heart rate is an objective measure of effort.**

- Pace varies with terrain, wind, temperature, fatigue
- Heart rate reflects actual physiological stress
- More consistent than pace for measuring effort
- Especially useful for beginners learning proper pacing

**Example:** 9:00/mile might be easy on flat ground but hard uphill. Your heart rate tells you the true effort level.

## Finding Your Max Heart Rate

### Method 1: Field Test (Most Accurate)

**Warm up thoroughly, then:**
1. Run hard for 3 minutes uphill
2. Jog 2 minutes recovery
3. Run hard for 3 minutes uphill (all-out)
4. Your max HR during the second effort is your max

**Note:** This is uncomfortable. Make sure you're healthy and have a training base first.

### Method 2: 220 - Age Formula

**Max HR = 220 - Your Age**

- Quick and easy
- Often inaccurate (±10-15 bpm variation)
- Better than nothing but not ideal

**Example:** 35 years old = 220 - 35 = 185 bpm estimated max

### Method 3: Check Your Watch Data

- Look at your highest recorded HR from hard efforts
- Needs several months of data
- More accurate than the formula

## The Five Heart Rate Zones

### Zone 1: Recovery/Easy (50-60% Max HR)

**Feel:** Very comfortable, can hold conversation easily

**Purpose:**
- Active recovery between hard workouts
- Warm-up and cool-down
- Recovery runs

**Duration:** 20-45 minutes

**Example (180 max HR):** 90-108 bpm

**When to Use:** Recovery days, warm-ups, cool-downs

---

### Zone 2: Aerobic/Easy (60-70% Max HR)

**Feel:** Comfortable, conversational pace

**Purpose:**
- Build aerobic base
- Most important zone for endurance
- Develop fat-burning efficiency
- Majority of training happens here

**Duration:** 30-120+ minutes

**Example (180 max HR):** 108-126 bpm

**When to Use:**
- Easy runs (most common)
- Long runs
- Most of your weekly mileage
- 70-80% of total training

**Key Point:** This should feel "too easy" if you're used to running hard.

---

### Zone 3: Tempo/Moderate (70-80% Max HR)

**Feel:** Moderate effort, can speak in short sentences

**Purpose:**
- Improve aerobic efficiency
- Transition zone between easy and hard
- Marathon race pace for many runners

**Duration:** 20-90 minutes

**Example (180 max HR):** 126-144 bpm

**When to Use:**
- Marathon pace runs
- Progressive long runs
- Some tempo work

**Warning:** Easy to drift into Zone 3 accidentally. For most training, stay below 70% or go into Zone 4 purposefully.

---

### Zone 4: Threshold/Hard (80-90% Max HR)

**Feel:** Comfortably hard, can only speak a few words

**Purpose:**
- Improve lactate threshold
- Most effective training zone for tempo work
- Build sustainable speed

**Duration:** 20-40 minutes total at this intensity

**Example (180 max HR):** 144-162 bpm

**When to Use:**
- Tempo runs
- Cruise intervals
- Lactate threshold workouts
- 10K to half marathon race pace

**Key Point:** This is your "comfortably hard" zone - sustainable but challenging.

---

### Zone 5: Maximum/VO2 Max (90-100% Max HR)

**Feel:** Very hard, can barely speak

**Purpose:**
- Improve VO2 max
- Develop speed and power
- Race-specific intensity for 5K and shorter

**Duration:** 3-8 minutes per interval (12-30 minutes total)

**Example (180 max HR):** 162-180 bpm

**When to Use:**
- Interval workouts
- 5K race pace efforts
- Hill repeats
- Speed work

**Recovery:** Need substantial recovery between intervals and after workout

---

## The 80/20 Rule and Heart Rate

**80% of training in Zones 1-2 (easy), 20% in Zones 4-5 (hard)**

**Weekly Example (5 hours total):**
- 4 hours in Zones 1-2 (easy runs, long runs)
- 1 hour in Zones 4-5 (tempo, intervals)

**Avoid Zone 3 for most training:**
- Too hard to build aerobic base
- Too easy to improve threshold
- "Moderate" training creates fatigue without clear benefits
- Exception: Marathon race pace can be Zone 3

## Using Heart Rate for Different Workouts

### Easy Runs
- **Target:** Zone 2 (60-70% max)
- **Rule:** If HR creeps into Zone 3, slow down
- **Note:** HR drift is normal on longer runs (gradual increase even at same pace)

### Long Runs
- **Target:** Zone 2, maybe low Zone 3 (60-75% max)
- **Rule:** Start in Zone 1-2, allow drift into low Zone 3 on long efforts
- **Note:** Duration matters more than pace

### Tempo Runs
- **Target:** Zone 4 (80-90% max)
- **Rule:** Should feel comfortably hard, sustainable
- **Note:** HR takes 2-3 minutes to stabilize, don't panic if it starts low

### Intervals
- **Target:** Zone 5 (90-100% max)
- **Rule:** Should reach high Zone 4/Zone 5 during work intervals
- **Note:** Recovery intervals should drop to Zone 2-3

### Recovery Runs
- **Target:** Zone 1-2 (50-65% max)
- **Rule:** Keep it truly easy
- **Note:** Often slower than you think

## Heart Rate Variability (HRV)

**HRV measures the variation in time between heartbeats.**

- **High HRV:** Well-recovered, ready for hard training
- **Low HRV:** Fatigued, stressed, need recovery

**How to use it:**
- Measure first thing in morning
- Track trends over time (not single days)
- If HRV drops significantly, take it easy
- Many watches and apps can track this

**Simple rule:**
- HRV normal or high = proceed with planned workout
- HRV significantly low = make it an easy day

## Factors That Affect Heart Rate

### Higher Heart Rate:
- Heat and humidity
- Dehydration
- Fatigue
- Illness
- Lack of sleep
- Stress/anxiety
- Caffeine
- Altitude

### Lower Heart Rate:
- Improved fitness (resting HR drops)
- Cool weather
- Overtraining (paradoxically)
- Certain medications

**Implication:** Don't be a slave to zones. Use HR as a guide, not an absolute rule.

## Limitations of Heart Rate Training

### Heart Rate Lag
- Takes 2-3 minutes to stabilize
- Not ideal for short intervals
- Better for steady-state efforts

### External Factors
- Weather, stress, sleep all affect HR
- Same effort may show different HR on different days

### Individual Variation
- Zones are estimates
- Your optimal zones may differ slightly
- Need to combine with perceived effort

### Cardiac Drift
- HR rises during long efforts even at same pace
- Normal physiological response
- Can make zone adherence difficult on long runs

## Combining Heart Rate with Other Metrics

### Heart Rate + Pace
- Track both over time
- Faster pace at same HR = fitness improvement
- Higher HR at same pace = fatigue or environmental factors

### Heart Rate + Perceived Effort (RPE)
- If HR seems wrong, trust your effort level
- Learn to recognize what each zone feels like
- Use HR to calibrate perceived effort

### Heart Rate + Power (Advanced)
- Running power meters are emerging technology
- Combines HR with mechanical work
- Most useful for trail/mountain running

## Practical Tips

### 1. Wear Your Monitor Correctly
- Chest straps more accurate than wrist
- Wet the sensors
- Snug but not too tight

### 2. Warm Up First
- HR takes time to rise
- Don't judge first 10 minutes

### 3. Look at Average HR
- Don't fixate on moment-to-moment variation
- Check average for the workout

### 4. Adjust for Conditions
- Hot day? HR will be higher at same effort
- Uphill? HR rises quickly
- Use zones flexibly

### 5. Track Resting Heart Rate
- Measure first thing in morning
- Decreasing over months = improving fitness
- Sudden increase = possible overtraining or illness

## Sample Training Week by Heart Rate

**Intermediate runner, max HR 180:**

- **Monday:** Easy 45 min, Zone 2 (108-126 bpm)
- **Tuesday:** Tempo: 10 min warmup Z1-2, 20 min Z4 (144-162 bpm), 10 min cooldown Z1-2
- **Wednesday:** Easy 40 min, Zone 2 (108-126 bpm)
- **Thursday:** Easy 40 min, Zone 2 (108-126 bpm)
- **Friday:** Rest or easy 30 min Z1 (90-108 bpm)
- **Saturday:** Long run 90 min, Zone 2 (108-126 bpm), allow drift to low Z3
- **Sunday:** Rest

**Total:** 80% Zone 1-2, 20% Zone 4

## Key Takeaways

1. **Zone 2 is your foundation** - Most training happens here (60-70% max HR)
2. **Easy should feel easy** - If Zone 2 feels hard, you're improving or need recovery
3. **Hard should be hard** - Zone 4-5 for quality workouts
4. **Avoid Zone 3 limbo** - Either easy or hard, not moderate
5. **Use HR as a guide** - Not an absolute rule, combine with perceived effort
6. **Track resting HR** - Best indicator of fitness and recovery
7. **Be patient** - Lower HR at same pace = you're getting fitter

**Remember:** Heart rate is a tool to help you train smarter. The goal is to learn what each intensity feels like, then run by effort while using HR to confirm you're in the right zone.
