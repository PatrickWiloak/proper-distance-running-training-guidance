# Progression and Periodization

Smart progression prevents injury and maximizes gains. Periodization organizes your training into phases that build on each other toward a peak performance.

## Progressive Overload

**To improve, you must gradually increase training stress over time.**

You can progress by increasing:
1. **Volume** (total mileage)
2. **Intensity** (workout difficulty)
3. **Frequency** (days per week)

**Critical Rule: Only increase ONE variable at a time.**

## The 10% Rule

**Don't increase weekly mileage by more than 10% per week.**

### Example:
- Week 1: 20 miles
- Week 2: 22 miles (10% increase)
- Week 3: 24 miles
- Week 4: 17 miles (recovery week, -30%)
- Week 5: 25 miles (resume building)

### Why 10%?
- Tendons, ligaments, and bones adapt slower than cardiovascular system
- You might feel capable of more, but injury risk increases sharply above 10%
- Conservative progression keeps you healthy long-term

### When to Break the Rule:
- Very low mileage runners (under 15 mpw) can progress faster
- Returning to previous training levels after time off
- Even then, be cautious

## Progression Phases

### Phase 1: Build Frequency First

**Goal: Establish running habit**

- Start: 3 days per week
- Progress to: 4-5 days per week
- All runs easy, 20-30 minutes
- Focus on consistency, not mileage
- Duration: 4-8 weeks

**Example:**
- Weeks 1-2: 3 days/week, 20 min each
- Weeks 3-4: 4 days/week, 20 min each
- Weeks 5-8: 4-5 days/week, 25-30 min each

### Phase 2: Build Volume (Mileage)

**Goal: Increase aerobic capacity**

- Maintain frequency (4-5 days/week)
- Gradually increase one run per week (the long run)
- Gradually extend easy runs
- Keep all running easy/aerobic
- Follow 10% rule
- Duration: 12-16 weeks

**Example:**
- Week 1: 5 runs of 30 min = 20 miles
- Week 4: 4x30 min + 1x60 min = 25 miles
- Week 8: 4x40 min + 1x75 min = 32 miles
- Week 12: 3x40 min + 1x50 min + 1x90 min = 38 miles

### Phase 3: Add Intensity

**Goal: Develop speed and lactate threshold**

- Maintain volume (or slightly reduce)
- Replace 1-2 easy runs with quality workouts
- Start with tempo runs
- Later add intervals
- Keep other days easy
- Duration: 8-12 weeks

**Example Weekly Structure:**
- Monday: Easy 40 min
- Tuesday: Tempo workout 50 min total
- Wednesday: Easy 30 min
- Thursday: Easy 40 min
- Friday: Easy 30 min or rest
- Saturday: Long run 90 min
- Sunday: Rest
- **Total: 40 miles, 2 quality days**

### Phase 4: Race-Specific Training

**Goal: Sharpen for goal race**

- Maintain volume
- Workouts become more race-specific
- Practice goal race pace
- Simulate race conditions
- Duration: 6-10 weeks before race

### Phase 5: Taper

**Goal: Arrive fresh and ready to race**

- Reduce volume 20-50%
- Maintain some intensity to stay sharp
- Prioritize rest and recovery
- Duration: 1-3 weeks depending on race distance

## Periodization: The Big Picture

**Periodization divides training into distinct phases, each with specific goals.**

### Macrocycle (6-12 months)
The entire training period from base building to goal race and recovery.

### Mesocycle (4-8 weeks)
Distinct training blocks with specific focus.

### Microcycle (1 week)
Individual training weeks.

## Classic Periodization Model

### 1. Base/Preparation Phase (12-16 weeks)

**Focus:** Build aerobic foundation

**Characteristics:**
- High volume, low intensity
- 90% easy running
- Build weekly mileage
- One long run per week
- No race-specific work

**Example Week (40 miles):**
- 5-6 easy runs
- 1 long run
- All conversational pace

### 2. Build/Development Phase (8-12 weeks)

**Focus:** Develop lactate threshold and aerobic capacity

**Characteristics:**
- Maintain volume
- Add tempo runs (1x per week)
- Still mostly easy running (70-80%)
- Progressive long runs

**Example Week (40 miles):**
- 1 tempo workout
- 4 easy runs
- 1 long run

### 3. Peak/Sharpening Phase (6-8 weeks)

**Focus:** Race-specific fitness

**Characteristics:**
- Maintain or slightly reduce volume
- Add intervals and race-pace work
- 2 quality workouts per week
- Workouts mimic race demands

**Example Week (38 miles):**
- 1 tempo or race-pace workout
- 1 interval workout
- 3 easy runs
- 1 long run with race-pace segments

### 4. Taper Phase (1-3 weeks)

**Focus:** Rest and recover for peak performance

**Characteristics:**
- Volume drops 30-50%
- Maintain some intensity
- Emphasize rest and recovery
- Trust your training

**Example Week (20-25 miles):**
- 1 short tempo or race-pace session
- 3-4 easy runs (shorter)
- 1 medium-long run
- Extra rest

### 5. Recovery Phase (2-4 weeks)

**Focus:** Physical and mental recovery

**Characteristics:**
- Low volume
- No intensity
- Cross-training okay
- Complete rest is fine
- Reset for next cycle

## Applying Periodization by Distance

### 5K to 10K Training Cycle
- Base: 8-12 weeks
- Build: 6-8 weeks
- Peak: 4-6 weeks
- Taper: 1 week
- **Total: 19-27 weeks**

### Half Marathon Training Cycle
- Base: 12-16 weeks
- Build: 8-10 weeks
- Peak: 6-8 weeks
- Taper: 1-2 weeks
- **Total: 27-36 weeks**

### Marathon Training Cycle
- Base: 16-20 weeks
- Build: 8-12 weeks
- Peak: 8-10 weeks
- Taper: 2-3 weeks
- **Total: 34-45 weeks**

## Common Progression Mistakes

### 1. Too Much, Too Soon
- Increasing mileage and intensity simultaneously
- Exceeding 10% weekly increase
- Adding workouts before building base

**Solution:** Progress one variable at a time, be patient.

### 2. No Recovery Weeks
- Building indefinitely without down weeks
- Leads to overtraining and injury

**Solution:** Every 3-4 weeks, reduce volume by 20-30%.

### 3. Skipping Base Phase
- Jumping into intense training without aerobic foundation
- Common when runners have limited time before a race

**Solution:** Always build base first, even if it means choosing a later race.

### 4. Random Training
- No plan or progression
- Doing whatever feels good that day
- Inconsistent stimulus

**Solution:** Follow a structured plan with clear progression.

### 5. Constant Peak Training
- Always doing hard workouts
- Never taking an off-season
- Leads to burnout and stagnation

**Solution:** Use periodization - hard phases followed by easier phases.

## Progression for Different Experience Levels

### Beginners (0-1 year)
- Focus on frequency and consistency first
- Build to 20-30 mpw over 6-12 months
- Mostly easy running for first year
- 1-2 races after 6+ months of base building

### Intermediate (1-3 years)
- Can handle 30-50 mpw
- Include 2 quality workouts per week
- Follow full periodization cycles
- 3-4 goal races per year

### Advanced (3+ years)
- 50-70+ mpw sustainable
- More sophisticated periodization
- Fine-tune training based on experience
- 2-3 major goal races per year

## Key Principles

1. **Progress gradually** - 10% rule for mileage increases
2. **Build base first** - Volume before intensity
3. **Take recovery weeks** - Every 3-4 weeks
4. **Use periodization** - Distinct training phases
5. **Be patient** - Fitness is built over months and years, not weeks
6. **Listen to your body** - Adjust progression if warning signs appear

## Sample Year-Long Training Progression

**Intermediate runner targeting fall marathon:**

- **Jan-Feb (8 weeks):** Off-season, 20-30 mpw, easy running
- **Mar-Apr (8 weeks):** Base building, 25-35 mpw, mostly easy
- **May (4 weeks):** Build phase, 35-40 mpw, add tempo runs
- **June (4 weeks):** Peak phase, 40 mpw, 5K race
- **July (4 weeks):** Recovery, 25-30 mpw, easy running
- **Aug (4 weeks):** Marathon base, 35-45 mpw, long runs
- **Sep (8 weeks):** Marathon build, 45-50 mpw, tempo + long runs
- **Oct (6 weeks):** Marathon peak, 50-55 mpw, race-pace work
- **Late Oct (2 weeks):** Taper, 30-35 mpw
- **Nov (Marathon race)**
- **Late Nov-Dec (6 weeks):** Recovery, 20-30 mpw

## The Bottom Line

**Smart progression and periodization allow you to:**
- Build fitness safely without injury
- Peak at the right time for goal races
- Avoid overtraining and burnout
- Sustain improvement over years, not just months

**Remember:** Running is a long-term sport. Patient, consistent progression beats aggressive, chaotic training every time.
